# Spanish speakers learning English — common pitfalls

## Phonology
- Prothetic /e/: Spanish speakers frequently insert an /e/ sound before words beginning with an 's' followed by a consonant, such as "espicy" for "spicy" or "estudent" for "student".
- /b/-/v/ merger: Because Spanish phonology does not distinguish between /b/ and /v/, learners often realize both as a /b/ sound, leading to pronunciations like "bery" for "very".
- /s/ vs /z/ devoicing: Spanish lacks the voiced /z/ phoneme in word-initial or medial positions, causing learners to devoice /z/ to /s/, as in "zoo" becoming "soo".
- Vowel length confusion: Difficulty with English tense/lax vowel pairs like ship/sheep or bit/beat leads to mispronunciations, as these distinctions do not exist in Spanish.
- Word-final cluster simplification: Learners often struggle with complex word-final consonant clusters, frequently simplifying them, such as "tasks" becoming "taks" or "next" becoming "nes".
- Stress-timed rhythm: Spanish is a syllable-timed language, and applying this to English leads to a lack of vowel reduction in unstressed syllables, making "today" sound like /tuˈdei/ instead of /təˈdei/.

## Morphosyntax
- Subject-pronoun drop: Since Spanish is a pro-drop language, learners may omit the subject pronoun, resulting in sentences like "Is raining" instead of "It is raining".
- Missing 3rd-person singular /-s/: The requirement to add an /-s/ to 3rd-person singular verbs is often forgotten, producing errors such as "He like pizza" instead of "He likes pizza".
- Age calque: Learners often translate the Spanish "tener años" literally, saying "I have 25 years" instead of the idiomatic English "I am 25 years old".
- Possessive adjective vs. article: Learners frequently use the definite article instead of a possessive adjective, saying "He broke the leg" when they mean "He broke his leg".
- Adjective placement: Influenced by Spanish syntax, learners may attempt to place adjectives after the noun, such as "the car red" instead of the correct "the red car".
- "It" vs. "There" existential: Learners may use "It is" for existence, producing sentences like "It is a man here" instead of the correct "There is a man here".

## Tense & aspect
- Present perfect vs. simple past: Learners often over-apply the present perfect to completed actions, saying "I have gone to the cinema yesterday" instead of "I went to the cinema yesterday".
- State verbs in continuous: Applying continuous forms to state verbs is common, leading to errors like "I am knowing him" instead of "I know him".
- "Since" usage: Learners may use the present tense with "since" to indicate duration, saying "I live here since 2010" instead of "I have lived here since 2010".
- Future intentions: Using the simple present instead of "going to" or present continuous, learners say "I go tomorrow" instead of "I am going tomorrow".
- Past tense "ed" pronunciation: Learners often over-pronounce the "ed" ending, making it a full syllable like "walked" as /wɔːkɪd/ instead of /wɔːkt/.
- Habitual past: Difficulty with "used to" leads learners to use the simple past inappropriately, such as "I played football when I was young" instead of the more precise "I used to play football".

## Articles & prepositions
- Definite article overuse: Learners often include the definite article with abstract nouns, saying "The life is hard" instead of the correct "Life is hard".
- "Depend of" calque: Spanish speakers often use "depend of" due to "depender de", whereas English requires "depend on".
- Missing prepositions with verbs: Common collocations like "listen to music" are shortened to "listen music" because the Spanish "escuchar música" lacks a preposition.
- "Married with": Using "with" instead of "to" in "She is married with him" is a common calque from "casarse con".
- Time prepositions: Learners often misuse time prepositions, such as "in noon" instead of the idiomatic "at noon".
- "Wait for": Omitting "for" in the phrase "wait for the bus" is common, resulting in "I wait the bus" due to the transitive nature of the Spanish verb "esperar".

## Word choice & false friends
- Assist ≠ Asistir: "Assist" in English means to help, whereas the Spanish "asistir" means to attend.
- Embarrassed ≠ Embarazada: "Embarrassed" means ashamed, but learners often mistake it for "embarazada," which means pregnant.
- Success ≠ Suceso: "Success" means achievement, while "suceso" means an event or incident.
- Actually ≠ Actualmente: "Actually" means in reality, while "actualmente" means currently.
- "Make" vs. "Do" collocations: Learners often use "make homework" instead of the correct "do homework," influenced by Spanish "hacer la tarea."
- Sensible ≠ Sensible: English "sensible" means reasonable or practical, while Spanish "sensible" means sensitive.

## Pragmatic / register
- Overformal "would you like": Learners often use "would you like to..." for simple requests, saying "Would you like to give me the salt?" instead of the more natural "Could you pass the salt?"
- Directness in imperatives: Spanish speakers may perceive English modal politeness as wordy, resulting in the use of direct imperatives like "Give me the pen" that can sound blunt in English.
- Overuse of "please": Learners may add "please" to every request to sound polite, but this can sound demanding if not accompanied by a modal verb like "could" or "would."
- Tag question misuse: Learners may add tag questions like "no?" or "isn't it?" to the end of all statements, attempting to force agreement rather than using them to solicit confirmation.
- Greetings: Translating "How are you?" literally in passing contexts can be perceived as too inquisitive when a simple "Hi" is the expected English norm.
- Response to "You're welcome": Learners often respond to "You're welcome" with "thanks" again, not realizing that "You're welcome" is the terminal point of the exchange.
