# Spanish speakers learning French — common pitfalls

## Phonology
- Spanish lacks nasal vowels; French /ɑ̃/ (an), /ɛ̃/ (in), /ɔ̃/ (on), /ẽ/ (un) require new articulatory targets: *pan* → *pain* /pɛ̃/.
- Spanish tap/flap /ɾ/ (*caro*) vs. French uvular /ʁ/ (*cher*): mispronouncing /ʁ/ as /r/ or /ɾ/ sounds unnatural.
- French distinguishes /y/ (*tu*) and /u/ (*tout*); Spanish only has /u/, so *tu* /ty/ often realizes as /tu/.
- Schwa /ə/ in French (*le petit* /lə pəti/) is often elided; Spanish speakers tend to pronounce all vowels: **le problème* → /lə pʁɔblɛm/ not /le pʁɔblɛm/.
- French *h muet* is silent (*homme* /ɔm/), while *h aspiré* blocks liaison (*les héros* /le eʁo/); Spanish *h* is always silent but has no liaison rules.
- Liaison and enchainement (*vous_avez* /vuzave/) create fluidity absent in Spanish, where word boundaries are clear.

## Gender & agreement
- Cognates often flip gender: *la leche* → *le lait*, *la sangre* → *le sang*, *la sal* → *le sel*.
- More gender shifts: *el mar* → *la mer*, *el color* → *la couleur*, *el dolor* → *la douleur*, *el calor* → *la chaleur*.
- Some Spanish masculine nouns ending in *-a* (due to stressed *a-*) are feminine in French: *el alma* → *l'âme* (fem.).
- Partitive agreement differs: *un poco de agua* → *un peu d’eau* (no agreement), but *algunas manzanas* → *quelques pommes* (agreement on adjective).
- Adjective position: many descriptive adjectives follow the noun in French (*une table ronde*) but precede in Spanish (*una mesa redonda*).

## Tense & mood
- Passé composé auxiliary: Spanish has no equivalent of *être* vs. *avoir*; remember *aller* → *je suis allé(e)*, *manger* → *j’ai mangé*.
- Subjunctive triggers differ: after *espérer que*, French uses indicative/future (*J’espère que tu viendras*), while Spanish requires subjunctive (*Espero que vengas*).
- French future stems are irregular (*je serai, tu auras, il ira*); Spanish regular endings can lead to errors like **je parleras* instead of *je parlerai*.
- Conditional formation uses future stem + imperfect endings (*je parlerais*); Spanish speakers may confuse it with the future.
- *Devoir* + infinitive (*je dois partir*) vs. Spanish *tener que* + infinitive (*tengo que irme*) for obligation.
- French *venir de* + infinitive (*je viens de manger*) corresponds to Spanish *acabar de* + infinitive (*acabo de comer*).

## Word order & negation
- Negation is two-part: *no como* → *je ne mange pas*; omitting *ne* or *pas* or misordering (**je mange ne pas*) is common.
- Direct object pronouns precede the verb in French: *lo veo* → *je le vois*; **je vois le* is incorrect.
- Inversion in questions: *¿Vienes?* → *Viens-tu?* or *Est-ce que tu viens?*; **Tu viens?* is colloquial, not standard.
- Adverb placement: *siempre como* → *je mange toujours*; **je toujours mange* is ungrammatical.
- Reflexive pronouns: *me lavo* → *je me lave*; **je lave me* is incorrect.
- Negative indefinite pronouns: *no como nada* → *je ne mange rien*; *nunca* → *ne...jamais*; *ya no* → *ne...plus*.

## Articles, partitives, prepositions
- Partitive articles are required: *quiero pan* → *je veux du pain*; *quiero agua* → *je veux de l’eau*; omitting them (**je veux pain*) is unnatural.
- *en* vs. *dans*: *en la casa* → *dans la maison*; *en Francia* → *en France*; *a Madrid* → *à Madrid*.
- To countries: *a México* → *au Mexique* (masc.), *a Francia* → *en France* (fem.), *a los Estados Unidos* → *aux États-Unis* (pl.).
- Prepositions with verbs: *pensar en* → *penser à* (*pienso en ti* → *je pense à toi*); *soñar con* → *rêver de*.
- *Acostumbrarse a* → *s’habituer à*; *empezar a* → *commencer à*; *casarse con* → *se marier avec*.
- *Sobre* (on/about) → *sur* (on) or *à propos de* (about); *sobre la mesa* → *sur la table*; *hablar sobre* → *parler de*.

## False friends and false cognates
- *constipado* (Es, having a cold) → *enrhumé*; *constipé* means constipated.
- *embarazada* (Es, pregnant) → *enceinte*; *embarrassée* means embarrassed.
- *restar* (Es, to subtract) → *soustraire*; *rester* means to stay.
- *lograr* (Es, to achieve) → *réussir*; *loger* means to lodge/rent.
- *ropa* (Es, clothes) → *vêtements*; *robe* means a dress.
- *tienda* (Es, shop) → *magasin*; *tente* means a tent.
- *piso* (Es, floor/apartment) → *étage* / *appartement*; *pis* is vulgar for urine.
- *carta* (Es, letter) → *lettre*; *carte* means a map or menu.
- *carpeta* (Es, folder) → *dossier*; *carpette* is a doormat.
- *pie* (Es, foot) → *pied*; *pie* in French is a pie (dessert).
- *paño* (Es, cloth) → *tissu*; *pain* means bread.
- *sobre* (Es, on/about) → *sur* / *à propos de*; no direct cognate.

## Pragmatic / register
- *tú* vs. *usted*: French *tu* is informal, *vous* formal; Spanish speakers may use *tu* too early or with strangers.
- French *vous* is also the plural pronoun, so *Comment allez-vous?* can be singular formal or plural; Spanish distinguishes *vosotros* (informal pl.) vs. *ustedes* (formal pl.).
- Politeness formulas: *por favor* is invariable in Spanish, but French requires *s’il vous plaît* (formal) or *s’il te plaît* (informal) matching the pronoun.
- Greetings: French *Bonjour* is used until evening (*Bonsoir* after), while Spanish switches *buenos días* → *buenas tardes* → *buenas noches*.
- Addressing strangers: in France, *Monsieur/Madame* is used more systematically than Spanish *Señor/Señora* in service encounters.
- Emails: French formal letters often open with *Madame, Monsieur,* and close with *Cordialement*; Spanish may be more direct.
