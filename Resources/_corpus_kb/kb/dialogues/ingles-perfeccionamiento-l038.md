## Original
1 Let’s imagine a person who is transported from the 1920s to today’s hi-tech world. (2)

2 Just think of all the new devices, processes and industries that have sprung up in the last half-century;

from energy-efficient jumbo jets to compact disks, from space shuttles to smart cards — (3) (4) (5)

a bewildering array of new and constantly developing products that have changed the way we live and speak.

The twilight industries of the industrial heartlands are swiftly being replaced by Silicon Valleys (and Glens) (6) (7),

manufacturing is increasingly making way for service industries,

and jobs that once were unthought of are now commonplace. (8)

Heart transplant surgeons, astronauts, computer programmers, rock stars,

10 fast food outlet workers or aerobics teachers are all inhabitants of the global village that our world has become. (9) (10)

English has become the global language, and the last few decades have altered it significantly (11)

because English is now the language of change and progress.

If our particular time traveller were British, he or she would have a lot to learn — and fast! (N3)

By the end of the second world war, American English had become the vehicle for conveying technological advances

in state-of-the-art industries such as avionics, telecommunications and computers. (12)

In our time traveller’s day, a manager (generally a man) would dictate a letter to his secretary (almost always a woman),

who would take notes in shorthand, type the letter, and send it by post to the firm’s subsidiary in New York.

The operation could take up to a week to complete. Today’s scenario is somewhat different:

the manager (man or woman) could dictate the letter into a dictaphone. A *text specialist* (generally a woman: some things don’t change!) (13)

19 would write the letter on a word processor, and send the final result down the telephone line,

using either a modem or a fax machine: the whole operation takes no more than ten minutes. (14)

The underlying need remains the same, but the procedure is radically different.

Technology, from computers to superconductivity, has changed the lives of most people in the developed world,

and efforts are also being made to apply new discoveries to the developing world. (15)

However, some people are worried about the dehumanizing effect of automation,

and the economic effect of more work being done by fewer people.

Only time will tell, but meanwhile we are perfectly justified in taking an admiring glance at progress over the last years.

Let’s follow our time traveller, who, like a fly on the wall, is following a typical day in the life of a dweller in the late twentieth century...

## Modernized
This lesson references technologies that have evolved significantly since publication. Modern equivalents include: *compact disks* → *streaming services*; *modem* → *broadband internet*; *fax machine* → *email/scanning apps*; *word processor* → *cloud-based document editors*; *dictaphone* → *digital voice recorders*. The core concepts of rapid technological change and globalization remain highly relevant.

## Lesson notes
- **Hi-tech**: contraction of *high technology* (cf. *hi-fi* for *high fidelity*). Writing words phonetically (hi for high, lo for low) is widespread in product names, especially in US.
- **Jumbo**: affectionate diminutive for elephant; often applied to products, especially large formats. *Jumbo jet* = largest jet aircraft. Verb *to jumboize* (horrific) = enlarge capacity to receive containers.
- **Disk/Disc**: both spellings exist; *disk* more common in US.
- **Smart**: intelligent. 1980s inventions: *smart card* (with microprocessor for account access), *smart munitions* (self-guided), *smart houses* (computerized energy-saving homes). Technology = *domotics* in Spanish.
- **Twilight industries**: *twilight* = dusk. Heavy industries (steel, etc.) called *smokestack industries* due to huge chimneys, now disappearing across Europe = *twilight industries*.
- **Silicon Valley/Glen**: Famous CA tech hub. In 1980s, IT industry (starting with Japanese firms) settled in Scotland due to low setup costs and skilled labor. Gaelic *valley* = *glen* (known to whisky lovers), hence *Silicon Glen*.
- **Unthought of**: *In my day, it was unthought of for a woman to go to work* = impensable. Maintain preposition *of*. Similar: *unheard of*.
- **Global village**: concept by Marshall McLuhan describing world interconnectedness.
- **State-of-the-art**: adj. compound = cutting-edge. *Our state-of-the-art technology is famous worldwide*. Also *leading edge technology*.
- **Decade**: 10 years. *State-of-the-art industries* = avionics, telecommunications, computers.
- **Modem**: modulation-demodulation; device to send digital data between computers via telephone. **Fax**: contraction of *facsimile machine* = telecopier.
- **Developing countries**: euphemism replacing *poor countries* or *underdeveloped countries*. Between *developed* and *developing*: *LDC* = Lesser Developed Countries.

## Exercises
**Sentence transformation:**
1. He’s really smart. He finished the crossword in three minutes.
2. Ten years ago, it was unheard of for a teenager to own a computer.
3. We have ten sales outlets in the State of Georgia alone.
4. The smart card contains state-of-the-art microprocessors.
5. When I’ve finished writing the report, I’ll fax it to you.
6. Exporting to developing countries can be a risky operation.

**Fill in the missing words:**
1. Cientos de empresas han surgido en el Silicon Valley. → Hundreds of companies ...... in Silicon Valley.
2. Entre las novedades encontramos unos motores cerámicos eficientes y unos «disquettes» de 3 pulgadas y media. → Recent developments include ....—......... ceramic engines and 3 1/2 inch floppy .....
3. Las industrias moribundas fueron rápidamente reemplazadas por las empresas de alta tecnologia. → ... industries were ....... replaced by ...-....
4. En otros tiempos, era inimaginable, ahora (ellas) son corrientes. → They were once ......... .., now they are
5. Existirá menos trabajo y menos gentes para realizarlo. → There will be .... work and ..... people to do it.

**Answers:** 1. Hundreds...sprang up 2. fuel-efficient...disks/discs 3. Twilight...swiftly... hi-tech 4. unthought of...commonplace 5. less...fewer
