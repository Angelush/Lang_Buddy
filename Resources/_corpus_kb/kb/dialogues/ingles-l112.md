## Original

1 Formas perifrásticas. — Son locuciones que no suelen corresponder entre sí, en los diversos idiomas. Así pasa entre el español y el inglés con expresiones como «tener que», «hacer falta», «tener necesidad», etc.; veamos algunos ejemplos.

Tengo que irme: I must go (leave).

Debes hacer (algunos) ejercicios: you must do some exercises.

¿Tiene usted todo lo que le hace falta?: do you have everything you need?

¿Qué más me hace falta?: what else do I need?

A veces podemos usar necessary. It is necessary to book: es necesario (hay que) reservar.

2 El pasado del condicional. — No debería plantear problemas. «Ella lo habría comprado»: Primero tomamos el condicional, she would, luego el auxiliar de pasado, have: she would have, y por fin el participio de pasado del verbo, bought: she would have bought it.

Con los verbos auxiliares y defectivos: «Tendría que habérselo dicho». El auxiliar de condicional es should: I should; el auxiliar del pasado, have: I should have, y luego el participio, «dicho», told: I should have told him. Habría podido ayudarme: he could have helped me.

3 Whatever: cualquiera que, cualquier cosa que, sea lo que sea... La traducción dependerá del contexto. «Whatever he says, he's wrong»: «cualquier cosa que diga» / «diga lo que diga», está equivocado.

Whoever: igual que above. Whoever phones, don't answer it: llame quien llame, no respondas.

Wherever: igual. Call me, wherever you are: llámame estés donde estés.

4 Tal. — Se traduce al inglés por such. Tenía tal acento que no podía comprenderle: he had such an accent that I couldn't understand him.

Cuando «tal» significa «por ejemplo» o «como», se traduce por such as. Le gustan los autores clásicos tales como Dickens: He likes classical authors such as Dickens.

5 Expresiones que conviene retener: 1 He'll be down in a minute. 2 We should have decided a month ago. 3 You've got more than you need.

4 Despite his money, he’s still unhappy. 5 She no longer works here. 6 How long will the job take? 7 The miners went on strike. 8 A doctor is available all night. 9 I don’t want to join a trade union. 10 Thanks for your help. — Not at all. 11 We always get lost in Paris. 12 I might buy a new one.

6 Traducción. — 1 Bajará dentro de un momento. — 2 Habríamos debido decidir hace un mes.

## Translation

1 Periphrastic forms — These are locutions that often do not correspond directly between languages. This is the case between Spanish and English with expressions like "to have to", "to need", "to be necessary", etc. Let's see some examples.

I have to leave: I must go (leave).
You must do some exercises.
Do you have everything you need?
What else do I need?
Sometimes we can use necessary. It is necessary to book: it is necessary to reserve.

2 The past of the conditional — Should not pose problems. "She would have bought it": First take the conditional, she would, then the past auxiliary, have: she would have, and finally the past participle of the verb, bought: she would have bought it.

With auxiliary and defective verbs: "I should have told him that". The conditional auxiliary is should: I should; the past auxiliary, have: I should have, and then the participle, told: I should have told him. He could have helped me.

3 Whatever: whoever, whatever, wherever... Translation depends on context. "Whatever he says, he's wrong" admits various translations: "whatever he says / whatever he may say / say what he may, he's wrong".

Whoever: same as above. Whoever phones, don't answer it: whoever calls, don't pick up.

Wherever: same. Call me, wherever you are: call me wherever you are.

4 Such. — "Tal" translates to such. He had such an accent that I couldn't understand him.
When "tal" means "for example" or "like", translate as such as. He likes classical authors such as Dickens.

5 Expressions to retain: 1 He'll be down in a minute. 2 We should have decided a month ago. 3 You've got more than you need.

4 Despite his money, he’s still unhappy. 5 She no longer works here. 6 How long will the job take? 7 The miners went on strike. 8 A doctor is available all night. 9 I don’t want to join a trade union. 10 Thanks for your help. — Not at all. 11 We always get lost in Paris. 12 I might buy a new one.

6 Translation — 1 He'll be down in a minute. 2 We should have decided a month ago.

## Lesson notes

Revision of Lessons 106 (1,2), 107 (2,5), 109 (2,3), 110 (2). Focus on periphrastic verb forms, conditional perfect, and compound relative pronouns (whatever/whoever/wherever). The lesson emphasizes idiomatic equivalence rather than literal translation. Practice identifying the most appropriate Spanish turn for each English expression.

## Exercises

1 Aunque le dije que no lo hiciera, insistió. — as I .... him ... to ... it he insisted.
2 Nunca he estado interesado en la historia romana. — I have ..... been interested ...... history.
3 Un bolígrafo es excelente para escribir rápidamente. — A biro is excellent ... ........

Respuestas: 1 Although - told - not to. — 2 never - in Roman. — 3 for writing quickly.

## Vocabulary highlights

- necessary: necesario
- need: necesitar, hacer falta
- should have: debería haber
- could have: habría podido
- whatever: lo que sea, cualquier cosa que
- whoever: quien sea, quienquiera que
- wherever: donde sea, dondequiera que
- such: tal
- such as: tales como
- to go on strike: ir a la huelga
- trade union: sindicato
- Not at all: De nada

## Patterns

- [periphrastic: have to / must / need] → tener que / hacer falta
- [conditional perfect: would have + past participle] → habría + participio
- [whatever/whoever/wherever] → lo que sea / quien sea / donde sea

## Agent cues

- Flag periphrastic verb clusters for special attention
- Convert conditional perfect constructions by chaining auxiliaries
- Recognize compound relative pronouns as single units
