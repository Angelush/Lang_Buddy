## Original

Twenty-fourth lesson

To tolerate... or to put up with?

The phenomenon we described in the last chapter is not just confined to nouns. The same process is used to transform verbs of classical origin into a combination of a simple verb and a preposition.
Thus ‘to tolerate’ will be used less frequently in everyday speech than ‘to put up with’,
and ‘to call in’ rather than ‘to visit’.
Listen to these two people:

— I can’t put up with his selfishness any longer. He either changes his ways or I go.
— Don’t get so upset. Why don’t you call in on your way back, then we can have a drink and a chat?

Unfortunately, the choice of verb and preposition is sometimes arbitrary, to say the least!

Listen to the following examples and try to understand the meaning of the ‘phrasal verb’ through the context which it is used in.

Government expenditure has been brought down considerably over the last three months in order to control inflation.
— How are you making out at your new job? — Well, the manager is a bit of a slave-driver but otherwise it’s fine.
After all the ups and downs of the last few weeks, share prices are beginning to pick up rapidly.
The United States has pulled its forces out of the area after a call from the UN Security Council.
The union is pushing for an increase in the basic wage and better overtime conditions.
— Could you put the meeting off until ten o’clock? I have to drop off my wife at the airport at nine.
— If you want to stay for a couple of days, we can put you up in David’s room. He’s still at university.
— You can’t rule out the possibility that they will refuse to cooperate with the management.

We will round off this lesson with a story about Marilyn Monroe.
A reporter asked her — Marilyn, what do you put on at night?
The star answered coyly — My perfume.

## Translation

Vigésima cuarta lección

¿Tolerar o «aguantar»?

El fenómeno que describimos en el último capítulo no se limita a los sustantivos. El mismo proceso se utiliza para transformar los verbos de origen clásico en una combinación de un verbo simple y una preposición.
Así, *to tolerate* se utilizará menos frecuentemente en el lenguaje diario que *to put up with*, y *to call in* en lugar de *to visit*. Escuche a estas dos personas:

— No puedo tolerar su egoísmo por más tiempo. O cambia su manera de ser o me marcho.
— No te enfades. ¿Por qué no pasas por aquí de regreso a casa para tomar una copa y charlar?

Desgraciadamente, la elección del verbo y la preposición es a veces arbitraria, ¡por decir lo menos!

Escuche los ejemplos siguientes e intente comprender el significado del *phrasal verb* dentro del contexto en que se utiliza.

Los gastos del gobierno han sido reducidos considerablemente durante los últimos tres meses para controlar la inflación.
— ¿Cómo te va en tu nuevo trabajo? — Bueno, el jefe es un poco esclavista, pero por lo demás todo va bien.
Después de todos los altibajos de las últimas semanas, los precios de las acciones comienzan a recuperarse rápidamente.
Estados Unidos ha retirado sus fuerzas de la zona tras un llamamiento del Consejo de Seguridad de la ONU.
El sindicato insiste en un aumento del salario básico y en mejores condiciones para las horas extras.
— ¿Podría posponer la reunión hasta las 10? Tengo que llevar a mi esposa al aeropuerto a las 9.
— Si desea quedarse un par de días, podemos alojarlo en la habitación de David. Él sigue en la universidad.
— No puede excluir la posibilidad de que se nieguen a cooperar con la dirección.

Terminaremos esta lección con una historia sobre Marilyn Monroe.
Un reportero le preguntó: — Marilyn, ¿qué se pone por la noche?
La estrella respondió con coquetería: — Mi perfume.

## Lesson notes

- **Phrasal verb necessity**: It is of primordial importance to learn the verb-preposition pair (or postposition) without seeking a logical reason. The smallest error can make the sentence incomprehensible; worse, it can lead to terrible misunderstandings!
- **Tolerate vs. put up with**: *To tolerate* is Latinate (formal); *to put up with* is the phrasal verb equivalent (informal). *She couldn’t put up with his arrogance* = *She couldn’t tolerate his arrogance*.
- **Ways**: *Ways* = customs, behavior. *You get used to his ways* = you adapt to his habits.
- **Call vs. call in**: *Call* = telephone (*Don’t call me before 10*). *Call in* = visit someone’s house (*Call in on your way back*). *Calling card* = business card.
- **Chat vs. chatter**: *To chat* = have a friendly conversation. *To chatter* = gossip (pejorative). *A chatterbox* = a gossip.
- **Slave/slave-driver**: *Slave* = person owned as property. *Slave-driver* = person who works others too hard. In technology, *slave* = mechanism controlled by another.
- **Pick up**: Literally = collect. In business = recover, improve. *Business picked up in December* = sales improved.
- **Pull out**: Withdraw. *He pulled out of the deal at the last minute*. *A pull-out supplement* = removable magazine section.
- **Push for**: Insist on obtaining something (journalistic). *He was pushing for a pay rise* = he demanded a raise.
- **Put off**: Postpone (*put off the meeting*). Also = discourage (*His attitude put me off*). *Put back* can also mean postpone.
- **Put up**: Accommodate someone (*We can put you up*). Not to be confused with *put up with* (tolerate).
- **Rule out**: Exclude. *We can’t rule out the possibility of failure*.

## Exercises

**Sentence practice**
1. She called in for a chat on her way home from work.
2. We’ve had a lot of ups and downs but we’re still married.
3. My new boss is a real slave-driver, to say the least.
4. Could you drop me off at the station? I can’t find a taxi anywhere.
5. You can’t rule out the possibility of a stock market collapse.

**Fill in the missing words** (all using verbs formed with *to put*):
1. His negative attitude completely discouraged me. → His negative attitude ______ ______ ______ completely.
2. We can accommodate you if you come to Portsmouth. → If you come to Portsmouth, we can ______ ______ ______.
3. Dress in warm clothes; it’s freezing outside. → ______ ______ some warm clothes; it’s freezing outside.
4. She couldn’t tolerate him any longer. → She couldn’t ______ ______ ______ him any longer.
5. Postpone the party for a few days until you feel better. → ______ the party ______ for a few days until you feel better.

**Answers**: 1. put me off, 2. put you up, 3. Put on, 4. put up with, 5. Put ... off

## Vocabulary highlights

- **tolerate**: accept something unpleasant (formal)
- **put up with**: accept something unpleasant (informal)
- **call in**: visit casually
- **slave-driver**: person who overworks others
- **make out**: manage, succeed
- **bring down**: reduce
- **control inflation**: manage rising prices
- **pick up**: recover, improve
- **pull out**: withdraw
- **push for**: insist on
- **put off**: postpone, discourage
- **put up**: accommodate
- **rule out**: exclude
- **drop off**: leave someone somewhere
- **ups and downs**: fluctuations, difficulties

## Agent cues

- **Phrasal verb focus**: Master the meaning and usage of each verb + preposition combination.
- **Contextual learning**: Derive meaning from context (e.g., *business picked up* = improved).
- **Formal vs. informal**: Contrast *tolerate* (formal) with *put up with* (informal).
- **Collocations**: Note common pairings (*push for a pay rise*, *rule out the possibility*).
