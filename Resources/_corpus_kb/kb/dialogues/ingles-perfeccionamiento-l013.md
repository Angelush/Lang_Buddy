## Original

1 Now that we have learned how to become rich, let's look at some expressions concerning money and business.

2 Over the Christmas period, the major stores do a roaring trade in consumer goods.
3 Despite a general business slump, electrical household goods are selling like hot cakes.
4 Unfortunately, another booming industry at this time of year is shop-lifting...

5 Consumer trends are changing. More and more people are turning away from the High Street
6 and doing their shopping in supermarkets, hypermarkets or huge cash-and-carry warehouses.

7 — How ever did you afford the new car? — The never-never, old man. I didn't have the ready cash so I bought it on HP.

8 A landscape in oils by Turner went under the hammer in New York last night. It fetched a record price of $30,720,000.

10 Now is the time to invest in blue chip stock. The market is still bullish but we are promised a bearish trend in the next few days.

11 That new computer store in Market Street has just moved into new premises. They must be making a bomb!

12 The saloon model is within your price-range but you'll pay through the nose if you want the sports version.

13 — I got ripped off when I bought my compact disc player. He charged me well over the odds.
14 — Well I wouldn't buy one just now. They cost an arm and a leg.
15 There was a small dent in the casing, so the salesman knocked off 10 %.

16 — He's a lousy manager and yet his company's finances seem in good shape. — Yes, he's got an intelligent sleeping partner.

17 That blender I bought from Marshall's broke down three times in a month. I got a real pig in a poke.
18 That new bistrot in St. James' Square is fantastic — but don't go there unless you're well-heeled. It costs a mint.

19 A company director was advised by his bank manager that he had an overdraft of £6,000 and that interest would be 22 % per annum.

20 The client insisted that he should deposit his Rolls Royce as security. He drove it to the bank and left it in the car park.
21 When he returned to pick up the car a month later, the bank manager asked him why he had insisted on leaving the car as security.
22 — Well I was off on holiday, came the reply, and where else can you get a month's free parking in Central London?

## Translation

1 Ahora que hemos aprendido cómo hacernos ricos, observemos algunas expresiones relacionadas con el dinero y los negocios.

2 Durante el período de Navidad, los grandes almacenes hacen un gran negocio con los bienes de consumo.
3 A pesar de un marasmo generalizado en los negocios, los electrodomésticos se venden como «rosquillas».
4 Desgraciadamente, otra industria en alza en esta época del año es la de robar.

5 Las tendencias de los consumidores cambian. Más y más personas se alejan de los comercios de High Street
6 y hacen sus compras en supermercados, hipermercados o en enormes almacenes cash-and-carry de comercio mayorista.

7 — ¿Cómo has podido pagar el coche nuevo? — Con el nunca-jamás, viejo. No tenía el efectivo, así que lo compré a plazos (HP).

8 Un paisaje al óleo de Turner se vendió en subasta en Nueva York anoche. Alcanzó un precio récord de 30.720.000 dólares.

10 Ahora es el momento de invertir en acciones preferentes. El mercado está todavía alcista, pero se prevé una tendencia bajista en los próximos días.

11 Esa nueva tienda de informática en Market Street se ha mudado a unos locales nuevos. ¡Deben estar ganando una fortuna!

12 El modelo berlina está dentro de tu presupuesto, pero pagarás un precio exorbitante por la versión deportiva.

13 — Me estafaron cuando compré mi reproductor de CD. Me cobró por encima de lo normal.
14 — Bueno, yo no compraría uno ahora mismo. Cuestan un ojo de la cara.
15 Había un pequeño defecto en la carcasa, así que el vendedor me hizo un 10 % de descuento.

16 — Es un mal gestor, y sin embargo las finanzas de su empresa parecen sanas. — Sí, tiene un socio capitalista inteligente.

17 Esa batidora que compré en Marshall's se averió tres veces en un mes. Me vendieron gato por liebre.
18 Ese nuevo bistró en la Plaza St. James es fantástico, pero no vayas a menos que tengas dinero. ¡Dejarás hasta la camisa!

19 Un director de empresa fue informado por su banquero que tenía un descubierto de 6000 libras y que el interés sería del 22 % anual.

20 El cliente insistió en dejar su Rolls Royce como garantía. Lo llevó al banco y lo dejó en el aparcamiento.
21 Cuando volvió a recogerlo un mes después, el director del banco le preguntó por qué había insistido en dejar el coche como garantía.
22 — Bueno, estaba de vacaciones, fue la respuesta, ¿y en qué otro sitio puedes aparcar gratis un mes en el centro de Londres?

## Lesson notes

**(1) Business**: Pronunciation /ˈbɪznəs/. From adjective *busy* /ˈbɪzi/. Pidgin English comes from deformation of *business*.

**(2) Over**: = *during*. *Over Easter, during Christmas*. No difference in meaning. *To roar* = to roar loudly, *roaring* = immense (familiar).

**(3) Selling like hot cakes**: = to sell very quickly and in large quantities.

**(4) To slump**: *He slumped in his chair* = se dejó caer. *Inflation rose due to the slump in the dollar* = baja del dólar. *World trade is in a slump* = el comercio mundial está en baja. Opposite: *boom*.

**(5) High Street**: Most English villages have a *High Street* (main street) for shops. In financial/business jargon: *the High Street* vs large companies. US equivalent: *Main Street*. *The price rises in the High Street have been lower than those in major pop makers*.

Alliteration (repetition of initial sounds): *cash-and-carry* is a good example. English is rich in these.

**(6) Never-never**: *To buy something on hire purchase (HP)* = buy on credit with option to purchase. *Leasing* = renting, no ownership at end. Both called *never-never* (never-ending payments) in familiar language.

*Cash-and-carry*: self-service where you pay cash and carry goods yourself. Simple! *Warehouse* = store, from *ware* (product, now only as suffix: *glassware, leatherware, hardware, software*).

**(7) Under the hammer**: to sell by public auction. *To come under the hammer* = to be sold. Suddenly we see the auctioneer's hammer. Verb: *to auction*. Person: *auctioneer*. *Auction sale* = public auction.

**(8) Stock Exchange vocabulary**: *Blue chip stock* = secure preferred stock. *Blue blooded* = aristocratic. *Bull market* = optimistic/buying market. *Bear market* = pessimistic/selling market. Origins lost in time, but think of a fierce bull vs. a hibernating bear.

UK vs US: In UK, *a bomb* = great success. In US, *bombed* = failed. *His latest film bombed*.

**(9) Making a bomb**: UK = making a fortune. US: failure.

**(10) Pay through the nose**: = pay the highest price. No dishonesty intended, just exaggeration.

**(11) Ripped off**: US slang, now worldwide. *To rip off* = to steal. Usually reflexive: *we got ripped off* = we were robbed/stolen from. Also: bad deal. Noun: *a rip-off*. *That new night-club's a real rip-off*. *Over the odds* = above normal.

**(12) Costs an arm and a leg**: US colloquial. UK: *costs a fortune*.

**(13) Lousy**: From *louse* (parasite). US: very bad. *What lousy weather!* *In good shape* = en buena forma (física o financiera).

**(14) Pig in a poke**: Old expression. At market, pigs sold in sacks (*poke* = old word, related to *pocket*). Buyer takes sack home, opens it, finds a cat. Meaning: bought something unseen, got a bad deal. More common: *to let the cat out of the bag* = reveal the secret. *We tried to organize a surprise party, but John let the cat out of the bag*.

**(15) Well-heeled**: = rich. *The Royal Mint* = Casa de la Moneda. *In mint condition* = like new. *That second-hand car was really cheap and in mint condition*.

**Mint**: costs a fortune. *Per annum* = per year (Latin, official contexts only).

## Exercises

1. Blue chip shares are selling like hot cakes at the moment.
2. Yes, and investors are paying through the nose for them!
3. Somebody must be making a bomb. — Yes. The stockbrokers.
4. I was sick for a month, but now I'm in good shape.
5. He slumped into an armchair and picked up the paper.
6. That old radio I bought is still in mint condition.

Fill in the missing words:

1. My mother-in-law is coming to stay___ Christmas. *(over)*
2. I wouldn't buy one just now. They're too expensive. *(wouldn't...just now)*
3. I tried to keep it a secret but she___ ___ ___ ___ ___. *(let the cat out of the bag)*
4. They___ ___ ___ a holiday this year. *(can't afford)*
5. The saloon is___ your price range. *(within)*
