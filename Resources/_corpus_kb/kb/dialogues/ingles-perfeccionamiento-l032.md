## Original

Thirty-second lesson

Journalists and politicians

— Hello Fred, how's life? I hear you're work-
ing free-lance these days. What's it
like? (1) (2)

— Couldn't be better: no more rigid dead-
lines, no more flak from the editor, and
no one going mad if you're late. (3)
What about you? Still writing a gossip
column? — Not likely! I'm covering the
election. (4)

— Me too. Let's swap notes. What do you
think of this Pearson bloke? Seems quite
liberal. (5)

— He's dyed-in-the-wool conservative, but
he'll climb on the disarmament bandwa-
gon (6)

if he thinks it will win him another few
votes. He makes no bones about it — he
admits it openly (7)

— What about Masters, the Christian De-
mocrat? He's above board, isn't he? (8)

— Not on your life! He was involved in that
slush fund affair with that big firm. (9)

He was in political limbo for a few years,
but even that doesn't make one iota of
difference. (10)

No one will go near him: his name's
mud. — I see. Well, Hamilton seems
honest enough. (11)

— You've got a short memory, haven't you?
Do you remember the last election?

He proposed a Bill to change the elec-
toral boundaries so that his party would
be re-elected.

— But that's blatant gerrymandering!
— Dead right. And what's more, he never
accepts any responsibility. (12)

He'll always pass the buck or look for a
scapegoat. (13)

— Oh Lord! Well, who's left? — Not much,
old chum. There's a new Ecology candi-
date. By and large, he's OK.

— He's a dark horse. No one's been able
to sound him out, but he's not got the
clout. (14)

— No, my young friend, it's Hobson's
Choice! (15)

## Lesson notes

(1) **How's life?** / **How are things?**: Fórmulas familiares de saludo (más británicas). En EE.UU.: *How are you doing?*.

(2) **free-lance** (adj./adv.): Trabajador independiente (especialmente en profesiones creativas). Origen: mercenarios medievales que vendían sus servicios (*free lance* = lanza libre).

(3) **deadline**: Fecha límite (originalmente, línea en la prisión más allá de la cual los prisioneros podían ser disparados). *rigid deadlines* = plazos estrictos. **flak**: Críticas (del alemán *Flugabwehrkanone*, defensa antiaérea; ver lección 29).

(4) **to gossip** = cotillear; *gossip column* = columna de sociedad; *gossip columnist* = cronista de sociedad.

(5) **bloke** = tipo/hombre (inglés británico; en EE.UU.: *guy*). **to swap notes** = intercambiar información/impresiones.

(6) **dyed-in-the-wool** = de pura cepa (originalmente de la industria de la lana: los hilos teñidos antes de tejer conservaban mejor el color). **bandwagon** = modas políticas (*to jump on the bandwagon* = unirse a una moda).

(7) **to make no bones about sth** = no andarse con rodeos: *He makes no bones about his past* = No oculta su pasado.

(8) **above board** = honesto/transparente. Origen: en los barcos, los oficiales comían "above board" (en cubierta, a la vista de todos), mientras los marineros comían abajo (en la bodega).

(9) **slush fund**: Fondo negro. Origen: en la marina, la grasa de la carne (*slush*) se vendía para hacer velas (ilegal pero lucrativo).

(10) **limbo**: Situación de incertidumbre. Origen religioso: *limbus* (lugar donde van las almas no bautizadas). *it doesn't make one iota of difference* = no cambia nada (referencia a la letra *iota* en un debate teológico del siglo IV).

(11) **your name is mud** = tu reputación está destruida. Origen: Dr. Samuel Mudd, médico que atendió al asesino de Lincoln (Abraham) y fue vilipendiado por asociación.

(12) **gerrymandering**: Manipulación de límites electorales para favorecer a un partido. Origen: Elbridge Gerry (gobernador de Massachusetts, 1812), cuyas circunscripciones parecen una *salamander* (un Equipo político + *salamander* = *gerrymander*).

(13) **to pass the buck**: Echar la culpa a otro. Origen: en el póker del Oeste, el *buck* (un trozo de plomo) marcaba el repartidor; al cambiar de turno, se pasa el *buck* al siguiente jugador. *buck* también = dólar (inglés estadounidense).

(14) **dark horse** = persona desconocida con potencial inesperado. Origen: carreras de caballos (un caballo desconocido que gana). **to sound sb out** = sondear a alguien. **clout** = influencia (del verbo *to clout* = golpear; argot político).

(15) **Hobson's Choice** = no tener opción real. Origen: Thomas Hobson (1631), dueño de una posada en Cambridge que alquilaba caballos por turno (el cliente tomaba el caballo que Hobson elegía).


## Exercises

1. Never listen to gossip; it may be about you.
2. — Hi, Mike, how are you doing? — Couldn't be better.
3. — Is Wilson above board? — Not on your life! He's a crook!
4. Lord, you've got a short memory!
5. — Who's left? — There's the Republican candidate, but he's a dark horse.
6. His name's mud.

Fill in the missing words:
1. Es un conservador de pura cepa. → He's a ... conservative
2. Eso no tiene ni una pizca de diferencia. → It doesn't make ... ... ... ...
3. El Presidente no puede desembarazarse de la responsabilidad. → The President ... ... the buck
4. El presidente de la Comisión del Senado es muy influyente. → The chairman of the Senate Committee ... ... a lot of ...
5. Los dos están rotos. Olivo y aceituno, todo es uno. → Both of them are broken. ... ... ...

## Patterns

| Form | Function | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Couldn't be better** | Maximum satisfaction | *—How are you? —Couldn't be better!* |
| **dyed-in-the-wool + adj.** | Thoroughly | *A dyed-in-the-wool conservative* |
| **climb on the bandwagon** | Join a trend | *He climbed on the eco-bandwagon* |
| **make no bones about** | Be frank | *She makes no bones about her age* |
| **above board** | Honest | *The deal was all above board* |
| **Not on your life!** | Absolutely not | *—Is he honest? —Not on your life!* |
| **Hobson's Choice** | No real choice | *It's Hobson's Choice: take it or leave it* |
| **pass the buck** | Shift blame | *He always passes the buck* |


## Agent cues

- **Political language**: The dialogue is rich in political idioms (*bandwagon*, *gerrymandering*, *clout*, *dark horse*), reflecting how politics is discussed in media circles.
- **Media-politics tension**: The text highlights the adversarial but interdependent relationship between journalists and politicians.
- **Irony**: *Not on your life!* and *Hobson's Choice* add humor to the political commentary.
- **Cultural note**: The lesson captures the cynicism often present in political reporting.
