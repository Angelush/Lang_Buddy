## Original

1 We've got a lot of nice things for you in the next few lessons.
2 — Stop!! How dare you be so sloppy with your vocabulary, for heaven's sake.
3 English is such a rich language — even a simple noise can be described in many different ways. Look...
4 The generator was humming softly in the corner of the room.

5 In the background you could hear the steady ticking of the huge clock.
6 A log fire crackled and hissed in the grate: it was too quiet!
7 Outside, footsteps clattered over the roof of the adjacent house.
8 Somewhere in the distance, a door banged shut. There was a rattle of a machine-gun
9 followed by a scream of pain. A heavy weight thudded into the bushes with a noise of snapping branches.

10 A torch snapped on behind him and he was dazzled by the powerful light.
11 He caught a glimpse of a tall dark figure disappearing into the night.
12 In the distance, he could see the tingling glow of the city lights.
13 Moonlight shimmered on the lake, while above his head the stars twinkled in the velvet sky.
14 He turned around, staring out to sea. A light winked in the darkness — a ship was coming into the harbour.
15 He shone his own torch onto his watch. Four o'clock. The gold watch strap glittered.
16 Pearls of dew glistened on the grass as dawn began to rise.
17 A triumphant gleam came into his eyes. — He's going to try and escape by sea. I'll catch him at the port.

## Lesson notes
- **How dare you...?**: A strong expression of disapproval or shock. *How dare you speak to me like that?* Note: *dare* is a semi-modal verb with two forms (regular and irregular). Idiomatic expressions: *I dare say you must be tired* = Supongo que estarás cansado.
- **Sloppy**: careless or messy. In British English, *a sloppy film* can mean a sentimental film.
- **Hum**: to make a low, steady noise; also to sing softly without words. *He was humming a tune* = Tarareaba una tonadilla.
- **Tick**: the sound a clock makes. *What makes him tick?* = ¿Qué es lo que le motiva/le hace funcionar?
- **Hiss**: an onomatopoeic word for a sharp, sibilant sound. Also used for the sound of a snake or an audience's disapproval (*the play was hissed*).
- **Grate**: the metal framework in a fireplace (pronounced like *great*).
- **Clatter**: a loud, rattling sound. *The horse's hooves clattered on the cobblestones* = Los cascos del caballo retumbaban en el empedrado.
- **Thud**: a dull, heavy sound. *The body thudded to the ground* = El cuerpo cayó al suelo con un ruido sordo.
- **Bang**: a sudden, loud noise (door, gunshot).
- **Rattle**: a rapid series of short, sharp sounds.
- **Snap**: to make a sharp, sudden sound; also to break suddenly. *The branch snapped* = La rama se quebró.
- **Pop**: a short, sharp sound (like a cork coming out of a bottle).
- **Fizz**: to bubble; the sound of bubbles. *Plop, plop, fizz, fizz... oh what a relief it is!* (famous US advertisement).
- **Shimmer**: to shine with a soft, slightly wavering light. *The road shimmered in the heat* = El camino temblaba de calor.
- **Twinkle**: to shine with sparkles that change constantly. Used for stars.
- **Gleam**: to shine brightly. *His eyes gleamed with anger* = Sus ojos brillaban de furia.
- **Glisten**: to shine with reflected light. *His teeth were gleaming white* = Sus dientes eran de un blanco reluciente.
- **Wink**: to close and open one eye quickly as a signal. A light can also *wink*. *A light winked in the darkness* = Una luz parpadeó en la oscuridad.
- **Glint**: a sudden, brief flash of light. *A glint of gold* = Un destello de oro.

## Patterns
- **Onomatopoeia**: Words that imitate natural sounds (*hum*, *tick*, *hiss*, *crackle*, *clatter*, *thud*, *bang*, *rattle*, *snap*, *pop*, *fizz*)
- **Sensory verbs**: Precise verbs for describing sounds (*hum*, *tick*, *hiss*, *crackle*, *clatter*) and light effects (*shimmer*, *twinkle*, *gleam*, *glisten*, *wink*, *glint*)
- **Vocabulary precision**: Using specific words instead of generic terms for accurate description

## Agent cues
Focus on the precision of English vocabulary for describing sounds and light effects. Practice: (1) using onomatopoeic words for specific sounds, (2) distinguishing between similar light verbs (*gleam*, *glisten*, *shimmer*, *twinkle*), (3) describing sensory experiences vividly. Note how each verb conveys a specific type of sound or visual effect. Pay attention to the difference: *shimmer* (soft, wavering), *twinkle* (sparkling, changing), *gleam* (bright, steady), *glisten* (reflected light), *wink* (brief, signal-like), *glint* (sudden, brief). Read onomatopoeic words aloud to capture their sound quality.
