## Original

Eighteenth lesson

Put it at the end...

— It looks like it’s going to rain. There goes our picnic. — That’s what I was afraid of.
— What are you looking for? — The plane schedule. I want to know what time we arrive.
— Did you know that Simon got married last month? — No. Who to?
— Don’t you like New York? — Sure, but it’s too exhausting to live in.
— I’d give you his address if I could find something to write with. — Here, use my pen.
— Many people are afraid to speak a foreign language because they hate being laughed at. — That’s no excuse.
— I saw that new psychological thriller at the movies last night. — Well, what was it like? — I don’t even know what it was about.
— What’s the matter? What are you crying for? — Nothing. I’ve just peeled two pounds of onions.
— I won’t be here on Saturday. Can we put the party off till next week? — Why do you want to put it off? We’ll have it without you.
— Don’t throw away the wrapper. There’s a coupon on it. — You know I never throw things away.

You see, it really is straightforward. It takes a little time to get used to, that’s all. There are of course a few difficulties: some verbs have two possible prepositions and both of them are correct. So take your time and re-read our examples regularly. Now let’s move on to something lighter.

The candidate had just finished a long, impassioned speech at a rally just before a crucial by-election. A late-comer slipped into an empty seat and asked his neighbour what the politician had said.
— I’ve no idea, came the reply. — Why? Weren’t you listening?
— Of course I was listening. For an hour and a half. But he never told us what it was about.

Putting prepositions at the end of a sentence can, however, prove disastrous. Take the example of the mother who promised to read to her son from a book of his choice.

The youngster chose a very serious volume on the wildlife in West Africa. Surprised by this sudden interest in nature, the mother said — What did you choose that book to be read to from for?
Work that out!

## Translation

Decimoctava lección

Colóquelo al final...

— Parece que va a llover. Adiós a nuestro picnic. — Eso era lo que me temía.
— ¿Qué buscas? — El itinerario de los aviones. Quiero saber a qué hora llegamos.
— ¿Sabías que Simón se casó el mes pasado? — No. ¿Con quién?
— ¿No te gusta Nueva York? — Sí, seguro, pero es demasiado agotador vivir allí.
— Te daría su dirección si pudiera encontrar con qué escribir. — Aquí tienes, usa mi bolígrafo.
— Muchas personas temen hablar un idioma extranjero porque odian que se rían de ellas. — Eso no es una excusa.
— Anoche vi ese nuevo drama psicológico en el cine. — Bueno, ¿y qué tal? — Ni siquiera sé de qué iba.
— ¿Qué pasa? ¿Por qué lloras? — Nada. Acabo de pelar dos libras de cebollas.
— No estaré aquí el sábado. ¿Podemos posponer la fiesta hasta la próxima semana? — ¿Por qué quieres posponerla? La haremos sin ti.
— No tires el papel de la envoltura. Tiene un cupón. — Sabes que nunca tiro las cosas.

Ya lo ves, es realmente sencillo. Requiere un poco de tiempo para acostumbrarse, eso es todo. Existen, por supuesto, unas pocas dificultades: algunos verbos tienen dos preposiciones posibles y ambas son correctas. Así que tómate tu tiempo y vuelve a leer nuestros ejemplos metódicamente. Ahora pasemos a otra cosa más ligera.

El candidato acababa de terminar un largo y apasionado discurso en un mitin justo antes de una elección parcial decisiva. Un asistente que llegó tarde se deslizó en un asiento vacío y le preguntó a su vecino qué había dicho el político.
— No tengo ni idea, fue la respuesta. — ¿Por qué? ¿No estabas escuchando?
— Por supuesto que estuve escuchando. Durante hora y media. Pero él jamás nos dijo de qué se trataba.

Colocar las preposiciones al final de la frase puede, sin embargo, ser desastroso. Tomemos el ejemplo de la madre que prometió a su hijo leerle el libro que él escogiera.

El joven eligió un libro muy serio sobre la vida en la selva del África Occidental. Sorprendida por este interés repentino por la naturaleza, la madre dijo: ¿Por qué has elegido ese libro para que yo te leyera un trozo?
¡Trate de entenderlo!

## Lesson notes

- **There goes...**: Indicates farewell to something cherished. *There goes our lie-in* = no more sleeping in. *There goes the new car!* = goodbye to savings.
- **Preposition omission**: English often drops prepositions in questions. *What time do we arrive?* (not *arrive at*). In statements: *I want to know what time we arrive* (no auxiliary *do*).
- **Get married to**: Preferred over *marry* when clarity is needed (since *marry* can mean to officiate: *He married his daughter*).
- **Sure**: American affirmative response (*Sure thing!* = Of course). British prefer *Of course*.
- **Pronunciation**: Tonic accent shifts in AmE vs. BrE: *my ADdress* (US) vs. *my adDRESS* (GB). Words starting with *ps-* or *pn-* (psychology, pneumonia) never pronounce the *p*: /saɪˈkɒlədʒi/, /njuːˈmoʊniə/.
- **Phrasal verb objects**: Nouns can go after (*put the party off*) or between (*put off the party*) verb and preposition, but pronouns MUST go between (*put it off*).
- **Throw at vs. throw away**: *Throw at* = direct toward (*threw the ball at the wicket*). *Throw away* = discard (*Don’t throw away old newspapers*).
- **Straightforward**: Easy to understand. Also means honest/direct when describing people.
- **By-election**: Special election to fill a vacant seat between general elections.

## Exercises

**Sentence practice**
1. What’s the matter with Alison? — She’s got pneumonia.
2. He decided to study psychology rather than law.
3. Have you seen these new throw-away razors? They’re really handy.
4. I have a terrible problem, Doctor, I’m in love with my psychiatrist!
5. Don’t you like your present? — Sure I do, but it’s a bit too small.
6. You’ll have to put off the party. — Put it off? Out of the question.

**Fill in the missing words**
1. ¡Caramba! Adiós a nuestro tranquilo fin de semana. → Damn! ______ ______ our quiet weekend.
2. ¿Por qué llora usted? → ______ are you crying ______?
3. Usted debe posponerlo inmediatamente → You must ______ ______ ______ immediately.
4. ¿No estaba escuchando? Seguro que sí. → Weren’t ______ listening? ______
5. Ambos están equivocados. — ¿Cómo? ¿Ambos de ellos? → ______ are wrong. — What? ______ ______ ______?

**Answers**: 1. There goes, 2. What ... for, 3. put it off, 4. you ... Sure, 5. Both ... Both of them

## Vocabulary highlights

- **lie-in**: sleeping late in the morning
- **plane schedule**: timetable for flights
- **sure**: affirmative (US colloquial)
- **address**: location of a place
- **psychological thriller**: suspense film focusing on mental tension
- **movies**: cinema (US; GB: *cinema*)
- **peel onions**: remove outer layers (causes tears)
- **wrapper**: packaging paper
- **coupon**: voucher, often for discounts
- **straightforward**: simple, easy to understand
- **by-election**: special election for a single position

## Patterns

| Form | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| There goes NP | bidding farewell to NP | There goes our picnic |
| put off | postpone | Put the party off |
| throw away | discard | Throw away the wrapper |
| call in | visit casually | Call in on your way back |
| get married to | marry (someone) | She got married to John |

## Agent cues

- **Preposition practice**: Note how English omits prepositions where Spanish includes them (*arrive* vs. *llegar a*).
- **Phrasal verbs**: Master *put off*, *throw away*, *call in*.
- **Cultural note**: *Movies* (US) vs. *cinema* (GB).
- **Listening focus**: Pay attention to stress patterns in AmE vs. BrE.
