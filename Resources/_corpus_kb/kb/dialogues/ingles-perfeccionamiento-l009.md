## Original

1 If you read that "the Queen Elizabeth arrived in Southampton yesterday",
2 you may be surprised to learn that the Elizabeth in question is an ocean liner.
3 The monarch would be referred to as Queen Elizabeth or King Charles, with no "the" to get in the way.

4 We will say that the United States and the Soviet Union are negotiating on arms control,
5 but we may add that Britain and West Germany are also participating in the talks.

10 The Italians and the French have also sent delegations to the conference.

Using the definite article the is a delicate business. Look at the following examples and see how it works:

I don't like coffee much, but the coffee they served us after dinner was horrible. It tasted like dishwater.

Dogs are usually a man's best friend but the Alsatian next door doesn't seem to have heard the news.

According to the opinion polls, you read in the papers, most people are in favour of the Common Market.

11 but most of the people we spoke to were extremely critical of the Community's agricultural policy and were suspicious of Europe.

Look again at the examples and notice that the definite article is used when the people or things referred to are, well, definite...

If they are abstract, we cannot use the. For example, there is an old saying which runs:

Capitalism is the exploitation of people by people. Socialism is the opposite.

However, we can represent a group of things or people by taking one to serve as an example:

"The horse is a noble animal" means that all horses are noble.

Woody Allen added a touch of cynicism to the Bible when he gave his own version of a famous quotation:

The lion and the lamb shall lay down together, he said, but doubting that the prophet was an animal-lover, he added: ...but the lamb won't get much sleep.

## Translation

1 Si usted lee que « la Reina Elizabeth (con artículo) ha llegado a Southampton ayer »,
puede sorprenderse al saber que la Reina Elizabeth en cuestión es un barco transatlántico.

Nos referiremos al monarca como Reina Elizabeth (sin artículo) o Rey Carlos, sin añadirle « the ».

Diremos que los Estados Unidos y la Unión Soviética negocian el control de armamento,
pero podemos añadir que la Gran Bretaña y la Alemania Federal participan también en las negociaciones.

10 Los italianos y los franceses han enviado también delegaciones a la conferencia.
Utilizar el artículo definido the es un asunto delicado. Observe los ejemplos siguientes y compruebe como funcionan:

No me gusta mucho [el] café, pero el café que nos sirvieron después de la cena estaba horrible. Sabía a agua de fregar platos.

[Los] perros son normalmente los mejores amigos del hombre, pero el perro alsaciano del vecino no parece haberse enterado de la noticia.

De acuerdo con las encuestas de opinión [que] usted lee en los periódicos, la mayoría de los personas están a favor del Mercado Común.

11 pero la mayor parte de las personas con quienes hablamos eran extremadamente críticas en cuanto a la política agrícola comunitaria y estaban recelosas de Europa.

Reconsidere los [nuevos] ejemplos y fijese que el artículo definido es utilizado cuando las personas o cosas de las que se habla, están, muy bien definidas...

Si son abstractas, no podemos utilizar the. Por ejemplo, existe una vieja expresión que dice:

El capitalismo es la explotación del hombre por el hombre. El socialismo es lo contrario.

Sin embargo, podemos representar a un grupo de cosas o personas tomando uno [de ellos] para que [nos] sirva de ejemplo:

«El caballo es un animal noble » significa que todos los caballos son nobles.

Woody Allen añadió un toque de cinismo a la Biblia cuando dio su propia versión de una cita célebre:

El león y el cordero dormirán juntos, dijo él, pero dudando que el profeta fuera un amante de los animales, añadió: ... pero el cordero no dormirá [por] mucho [tiempo].

## Lesson notes

**(1) Way**: A versatile word meaning 'road' or 'path', but used in many contexts. 'WAY IN' and 'WAY OUT' signify entrance/exit. 'To be in the way' or 'to get in the way' = to obstruct. *Be careful when you move the bookcase. The chair's in the way. I've got a lot of work to do so be quiet and don't get in my way.*

**(2) Plural countries, singular verbs**: While 'The United States' and 'The Soviet Union' imply plurality, they take singular verbs because they refer to a single country. *The United States is on home ground and the Soviet Union doesn't like to be in a position of inferiority.*

**(3) West/East Germany**: West Germany = Federal Republic of Germany (FRG), East Germany = German Democratic Republic (GDR).

**(4) Washing dishes**: UK: *to wash up* = to wash dishes. US: *to do the dishes*. In US English, *to wash up* = personal hygiene. Dishwashing liquid: *washing-up liquid* (UK) / *dish soap* (US). Dishwasher: *the dish-washer*.

**(5) Alsatian**: German Shepherd breed renamed in UK due to WWI anti-German sentiment. Similarly, British royal family changed name from Saxe-Coburg to Windsor.

**(6) Poll**: Two uses: opinion poll (survey) and voting poll. From Dutch for 'head'. *To go to the polls* = to vote. *A straw poll* = unofficial pre-election survey. *A pollster* = person organizing polls.

**(7) Intonation**: English uses stress to convey meaning other languages need full phrases for. Notice stress on *we* in: *we spoke to were extremely critical...*. This is extremely common.

**(8) Person vs persons**: Plural of 'person' is usually *people*. *Persons* used in official contexts (documents, signs).

**(9) The Bible**: Takes *the* because it's unique.

**(10) Woody Allen**: NY humorist/director. Parodies prophet Isaiah who predicted peace and wild beasts becoming docile.

## Exercises

1. You can't rush things! Getting divorced is a delicate business.
2. Many people are highly suspicious of opinion polls.
3. He doesn't seem to have heard the news that the war is over!
4. Please move that stool out of the way.
5. Apparently, one out of four people own a car, but most of the people I know don't even have a bike!
6. Huh! What do you know about politics? You don't even vote!

Fill in the missing words:

We have left a blank space in front of the nouns. Place a definite article where appropriate.

1. He lives in___ Netherlands and his cousin lives in___ West Germany. *(The, -)*
2. I loved___ wine they served with the fish course. *(the)*
3. I don't trust___ opinion polls, but___ poll they published yesterday was really telling. *(-, the)*
4. He's got definite views. He believes in___ capitalism and___ Bible. *(-, the)*
5. ___lions are generally difficult to photograph but___ lions in this safari park are very obliging! *(-, the)*

## Vocabulary highlights

- **ocean liner**: transatlántico
- **monarch**: monarca
- **United States/Soviet Union**: países con forma plural (llevan *the*)
- **Britain/West Germany**: países sin artículo
- **opinion poll**: encuesta de opinión
- **Common Market**: Mercado Común
- **Alsatian**: pastor alemán (perro)
- **capitalism/socialism**: capitalismo/socialismo
- **Bible**: la Biblia (siempre con *the*)
- **to get in the way**: estorbar

## Patterns

- definite-article-proper-nouns: *The* with certain proper nouns (*the United States*), none with others (*Britain*)
- abstract-vs-concrete: No article for abstract nouns in general statements (*Capitalism is...*), *the* for specific instances
- general-vs-specific: General (*Dogs are...*) vs. specific (*the Alsatian next door*)
- the-way-phrases: *To be in the way / to get in the way*
- dish-washing-terms: UK *to wash up* vs. US *to do the dishes*
