## Original

Twenty-third lesson

It’s all Greek to me

— Malcolm is newly employed. — Indeed? What is the amount of his pecuniary remuneration?
Stop! Amazingly enough, these two people are speaking English, but a sort of English we could baptise ‘officialese’.

Remember we told you that Norman French, of Latin origin, was grafted onto the native tongue of the British, of Germanic origin.
Well, the parallel vocabulary syndrome means that, quite often, we write one word and say another — for the same thing!
Listen to the exchange at the beginning of the lesson, ‘translated’ into everyday speech!
Malcolm’s got a new job. — Really? How much does he earn? Phew! That’s clearer, isn’t it?

The English language has absorbed these ‘intellectual’ words alongside their more proletarian counterparts and this can prove confusing to a person who has no classical education because the output of government administrations, law, science and technology is written in this form of the tongue we have called officialese.

Things came to such a head a few years ago that a proposal was considered to introduce Latin into the primary classroom — not for lofty academic reasons but so that the young adult would be equipped to understand this gobbledegook.

If an optician were to tell his patient that he or she was myopic, the poor person probably would not understand his condition. A dictionary would tell him that he was short-sighted. Or it could have been daltonism. What! What on earth is that? Is it fatal? Relax, it only means that you’re colour-blind. You don’t need a white stick and a canine quadruped, sorry, a dog.

It is important to understand this ‘explaining’ tendency of the English language — the practice of breaking down intellectual words into their basic parts, generally using words of Germanic origin.
If you want to understand the ordinary native English speaker rather than the stodgy official texts which appear all too regularly, learn not to use words which are sesquipedalian, sorry, too long!

## Translation

Vigésima tercera lección

Esto es griego para mí

— Malcolm se ha empleado recientemente. — ¿De veras? ¿Cuál es el montante de su remuneración pecuniaria?
¡Alto! Por sorprendente que parezca, esas dos personas hablan inglés, pero un tipo de inglés que pudiéramos bautizar «officialese».

Recuerde que le dijimos que el francés normando, de origen latino, fue incorporado a la lengua nativa de los británicos, de origen germánico.
Pues bien, el síndrome del vocabulario paralelo quiere decir que, con bastante frecuencia, escribimos una palabra y decimos otra — ¡para expresar la misma cosa!
¡Escuche el diálogo al principio de la lección, «traducido» al lenguaje diario!
— Malcolm tiene un nuevo trabajo. — ¿De verdad? ¿Cuánto gana? ¡Phew! Eso es más claro, ¿no es cierto?

El idioma inglés ha absorbido estos términos «intelectuales» conjuntamente con sus equivalentes más «proletarios», y esto puede confundir a una persona que carezca de educación clásica, debido a que el lenguaje de las administraciones gubernamentales, jurídicas, científicas y tecnológicas está escrito en esta forma de idioma que hemos llamado «officialese».

Las cosas llegaron hasta tal punto hace unos años que se pensó en una proposición para introducir el latín en las aulas de las clases primarias — no por razones específicamente académicas, sino con el fin de que los jóvenes adultos estuvieran preparados para comprender este galimatías.

Si un óptico debiera decir a su cliente que él o ella era miope, esta desdichada persona probablemente no entendería su problema. Un diccionario le diría que es *short-sighted*. ¡O que pudiera ser *daltonism*! ¡Qué! ¿Qué es eso? ¿Es mortal? Relájese, eso quiere decir que usted es *colour-blind*. Usted no necesita un bastón blanco ni un cuadrúpedo canino, perdón, un perro.

Es importante comprender esta tendencia «a explicar» del idioma inglés — la práctica de «descomponer» las palabras intelectuales en sus componentes básicos, empleando generalmente palabras de origen germánico.
Si usted quiere comprender al usuario normal del inglés en lugar de los abrumadores textos oficiales que aparecen con demasiada regularidad, ¡aprenda a no utilizar las palabras que son sesquipedales, perdón, demasiado largas!

## Lesson notes

- **Officialese/journalese/legalese**: Terms for specialized jargon, often with *-ese* suffix (slightly pejorative).
- **Norman French/Germanic origins**: English has a dual heritage. After 1066, Norman French (Latin-based) was added to Old English (Germanic). This creates pairs like *start* (Germanic) / *commence* (Latin), *freedom* / *liberty*.
- **Parallel vocabulary**: Many concepts have both a Latinate (formal) and Germanic (informal) term. Written English prefers Latin; spoken English prefers Germanic.
- **Classical vs. classic**: *Classical* = related to ancient Greece/Rome or the classical period (18th century). *Classic* = something excellent/timeless (*a classic car*).
- **Come to a head**: Reach a critical point. *The problem came to a head when they published the article*.
- **Lofty**: Originally ‘high’; now abstract for noble ideals. *He has lofty ideals*. *A loft* = attic apartment.
- **Gobbledegook**: Pompous, incomprehensible language (US origin). *I can’t understand all that gobbledegook on TV!*
- **Myopic/short-sighted**: Near-sighted. *Myopic* is more technical; *short-sighted* is common.
- **Daltonism/colour-blind**: Color blindness. *Daltonism* is technical (named after scientist John Dalton); *colour-blind* is common. *What on earth?* = exclamation of surprise (less strong than *What the hell?*).
- **Canine quadruped**: Humorous periphrasis for *dog*. *Quadruped* = four-legged animal.
- **Sesquipedalian**: Long word (literally ‘a foot and a half long’). Used humorously.
- **Stodgy**: Heavy, indigestible (food); overloaded (style). *Stodgy text* = hard to understand.

## Exercises

**Sentence practice**
1. Who is his counterpart in the Ministry of Trade?
2. Things came to such a head that the manager resigned.
3. I can’t understand this gobbledegook! Write simply!
4. Why do you need glasses? Are you short-sighted or long-sighted?
5. — What on earth is daltonism? — It just means you’re colour-blind.
6. His Russian is good, but he’s not a native speaker.

**Fill in the missing words**
1. ¿Le gusta a usted la música clásica o prefiere el rock? → Do you like ______ music or do you prefer rock?
2. La bancarrota llega con demasiado frecuencia. → Bankruptcy happens ______ ______ ______.
3. Por sorprendente que parezca, ¡ella es una verdadera princesa! → ______ ______, she really is a princess!
4. Los dos idiomas existen uno al lado del otro. → The two languages exist ______ ______ ______.
5. Los textos oficiales pueden a menudo prestarse a confusión. → Official texts can often ______ ______.

**Answers**: 1. classical, 2. all too often, 3. Amazingly enough, 4. alongside each other, 5. prove confusing

## Vocabulary highlights

- **officialese**: bureaucratic language
- **pecuniary remuneration**: payment for work (formal)
- **Norman French**: French variety brought to England in 1066
- **Germanic origin**: Old English roots
- **parallel vocabulary**: Latin vs. Germanic word pairs
- **syndrome**: set of characteristics
- **intellectual/proletarian**: erudite vs. common terms
- **classical education**: study of Greek/Latin
- **gobbledegook**: gibberish
- **optician**: eye doctor (prescribes glasses)
- **myopic/short-sighted**: near-sighted
- **daltonism**: color blindness (technical)
- **colour-blind**: unable to distinguish colors
- **canine quadruped**: dog (humorous)
- **sesquipedalian**: overly long (words)

## Agent cues

- **Register awareness**: Distinguish formal (*commence*, *remuneration*) from informal (*start*, *pay*).
- **Etymology**: Recognize Latin vs. Germanic word pairs.
- **Jargon identification**: Spot *officialese*, *gobbledegook* in texts.
- **Humor**: Appreciate the irony in *canine quadruped* vs. *dog*.
