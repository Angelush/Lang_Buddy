## Original

1 A Frenchman decided to adopt British citizenship. After the ceremony, which was very moving, the clerk asked him if he was proud of his new nationality. The ex-Frenchman replied:
2 — Of course I am. You see, he said in a confidential tone, now I can say that we won the Battle of Waterloo.

3 A couple of Polish émigrés to the United States had been waiting for ten years to be granted American nationality.
4 One day, the husband came running into the kitchen, waving a letter and shouting — We're Americans! We're Americans!
5 His wife turned, took off her apron and said — How wonderful! Now you can wash the dishes!

6 When asked if his fellow countrymen were chauvinistic, an American economist replied:
7 — Yes and no. An American is someone who wears English suits, Italian shoes and French silk ties.
8 He drinks Brazilian coffee, eats Swiss cheese with Austrian bread, and drives a German car.
9 Then he takes a Japanese ballpoint pen and Chinese notepaper to write to his congressman,
10 demanding that he do something about the foreign companies taking away our foreign markets.

11 Americans have a reputation for being wealthy and generous tourists — not always willingly...
12 An American tourist stopped in a tiny French village for lunch at the local inn.
13 He ordered an omelette, which was delicious. But when the bill came, he got the shock of his life.
14 — Tell me, he asked the owner of the restaurant, are eggs scarce in this part of the world?
15 — No, replied the owner, but Americans are!

16 One problem that faces the British when they visit the USA is what to call "the smallest room in the house".
17 The Americans shudder at the word "toilet"; they call it the bathroom, which for English people is where you go to wash your hands or take a bath.
18 The problem is illustrated by the following true story:
19 In a very chic Manhattan restaurant, an Englishman visiting the USA for the first time asked the head waiter:
20 — Excuse me, my man, where are the toilets? The waiter was outraged and replied in a haughty tone:
21 — Go up the stairs, turn right and go to the back of the room. On your left you will see a door marked "Gentlemen".
22 But pay no attention. You can go in despite everything.

23 Not all Americans are proud of their discoverer Christopher Columbus, although there is a national holiday named after him.
24 When he set off, he did not know where he was going; when he arrived, he did not know where he was;
25 and when he returned, he could not tell anybody where he had been! Some heritage!

## Translation

1 Un francés decidió adoptar la ciudadanía británica. Después de la ceremonia, la cual fue muy emotiva, el empleado le preguntó si estaba orgulloso de su nueva nacionalidad. El ex-francés respondió:
2 — Por supuesto que sí. Vea usted, dijo en un tono confidencial, ahora puedo decir que nosotros ganamos la batalla de Waterloo.

3 Una pareja de emigrantes polacos a los Estados Unidos ha estado esperando durante diez años para que le sea concedida la nacionalidad norteamericana.
4 Un día, el marido vino corriendo a la cocina, agitando una carta y gritando — ¡Somos americanos! ¡Somos americanos!
5 Su mujer se dio la vuelta, se quitó el delantal y dijo — ¡Qué maravilla! ¡Ahora puedes lavar los platos tú!

6 Cuando se le preguntó que si sus compatriotas eran chauvinistas, un economista americano respondió:
7 — Sí y no. Un americano es alguien que usa trajes ingleses, zapatos italianos y corbatas francesas de seda.
8 Bebe café brasileño, come queso suizo con pan austriaco, y conduce un coche alemán.
9 Luego coge un bolígrafo japonés y papel de carta chino para escribir a su diputado,
10 y pedirle que haga algo con relación a las compañías extranjeras que están arrebatándonos nuestros mercados extranjeros.

11 Los americanos tienen fama de ser turistas ricos y generosos (aunque no siempre voluntariamente...).
12 Un turista americano se detuvo en una minúscula aldea francesa para comer en una posada local.
13 Pidió una tortilla, que estaba deliciosa. Pero cuando le trajeron la cuenta, se llevó el susto de su vida.
14 — Dígame, preguntó al propietario del restaurante, ¿hay escasez de huevos en esta parte del mundo?
15 — De huevos, no, respondió el propietario, ¡pero de turistas americanos, sí!

16 Un problema que los británicos confrontan cuando visitan EU es cómo llamar «a la habitación más pequeña de la casa».
17 Los americanos se horrorizan ante la palabra «toilet»; ellos le llaman «el baño» (el bathroom), lo que para los ingleses es el lugar donde uno va a lavarse las manos o a tomar un baño.
18 El problema queda ilustrado por esta auténtica historieta:
19 En un elegante restaurante de Manhattan, un inglés que visitaba EU por primera vez le preguntó al camarero jefe:
20 — Perdóneme, amigo mío, ¿dónde están los aseos? El camarero se escandalizó y respondió en un tono arrogante:
21 — Suba las escaleras, gire a la derecha y camine hasta el fondo del salón. A su izquierda verá una puerta con un rótulo que dice «Caballeros».
22 Pero, descuide usted. Puede entrar a pesar de todo.

23 No todos los americanos están orgullosos de su descubridor Cristóbal Colón, aunque exista un día de fiesta nacional que se celebra en su nombre.
24 Cuando este partió, no sabía a dónde se dirigía; cuando llegó, no sabía dónde estaba;
25 ¡y cuando regresó, no pudo decirle a nadie dónde había estado! ¡Qué herencia!

## Lesson notes
- **Clerk**: A versatile word meaning office worker, shop assistant, etc. A *bank clerk* = bank employee. Note pronunciation: British /klɑːk/, American /klɜːrk/. Listen to the audio for the difference.
- **To grant**: to give or allow something officially. *India was granted independence in 1947.* A *grant* = a scholarship or subsidy. *The government is cutting student grants this year.*
- **Confidential tone**: a private, secret manner of speaking.
- **Battle of Waterloo**: fought in 1815, where Napoleon was defeated by the British and Prussian armies. Note the cultural significance: the Frenchman can now say *we* won (as a British citizen).
- **Émigré**: a person who has left their country for political reasons.
- **To wave**: to move something back and forth. *He waved a letter* = agitaba una carta.
- **Apron**: a garment worn over the front of the body to protect clothes.
- **Chauvinistic**: showing excessive or blind nationalism. From Nicolas Chauvin, a fictional French soldier.
- **To set off**: to begin a journey. Also *to set out*.
- **Scarce**: not plentiful. *Scarcely* = barely. Note pronunciation: /skɛəs/, /ˈskɛəsli/. *Eggs are scarce* = los huevos escasean.
- **To shudder**: to tremble with fear or disgust.
- **Bathroom vs. Toilet vs. Restroom**: In British English, *toilet* is most common for the WC. In American English, *bathroom* is used (though it originally meant a room with a bath). For public places, Americans use *restroom*. British informal: *loo*.
- **Columbus Day**: October 8th, commemorating Columbus's arrival in the Bahamas in 1492. Note: Columbus was Italian, sailing for Spain.
- **Some...!**: used ironically in exclamations. *Some holiday! It rained every day!* = ¡Vaya vacaciones! ¡Ha llovido todos los días!
- **All the same**: despite everything; nevertheless. *He told me not to go, but I went all the same.*
- **Make a decision (US) / Take a decision (UK)**: different expressions for the same action. *Decision-makers* is used in both.
- **Different from/to/than**: as noted in Lesson 44, *from* is most widely accepted; *to* is British; *than* is American.

## Exercises
1. If you need a receipt you'll have to ask the desk clerk.
2. Of course it's a nice restaurant: you don't have to pay the bill!
3. Because of the economic crisis, money is scarce at the moment.
4. Pay no attention to the sign. Turn left all the same.
5. Pack your bags tonight because I want to leave very early in the morning.
6. Some picnic: we stayed in the car and ate a hamburger!

Fill in the missing words:
1. I was so angry that I could scarcely answer ...... (the question).
2. He came running into the kitchen, waving ...... (a letter).
3. His mother is ......... and his father is ......... (Brazilian, Austrian).
4. Americans have a reputation for being ......... and ......... (wealthy, generous).
5. When the bill came, they got ......... ......... ......... ......... (the shock of their lives).

- **Citizenship**: Status of being a citizen of a country
- **Ceremony**: Formal event or ritual
- **Clerk**: Office worker or shop assistant
- **Moving**: Emotional; causing strong feelings
- **Confidential**: Private or secret
- **Battle of Waterloo**: Historic 1815 battle where Napoleon was defeated
- **Émigré**: Political refugee or exile
- **Nationality**: Citizenship or national identity
- **Granted**: Given or awarded officially
- **Wave**: To move back and forth as a signal
- **Apron**: Protective garment worn over clothes
- **Chauvinistic**: Excessively or blindly patriotic
- **Suit**: Formal jacket and trousers/skirt
- **Tie**: Neckwear
- **Shoes**: Footwear
- **Coffee/Cheese/Bread/Car**: Cultural products from different countries
- **Ballpoint**: Type of pen with a small rolling ball
- **Notepaper**: Writing paper, often decorative
- **Congressman**: US elected legislative representative
- **Foreign**: From or characteristic of another country
- **Companies**: Business organizations
- **Markets**: Commercial systems or areas
- **Reputation**: Public opinion or standing
- **Tourist**: Person traveling for pleasure
- **Omelette**: Dish made from beaten eggs cooked in a pan
- **Bill**: Invoice or list of charges
- **Shock**: Sudden surprise or emotional impact
- **Scarcely**: Barely or almost not
- **Scarce**: In short supply
- **Toilet**: British term for WC (water closet)
- **Bathroom**: American term for room with bath and/or toilet
- **Restroom**: American term for public toilet
- **Columbus**: Christopher Columbus, explorer
- **Heritage**: Cultural legacy or inheritance
- **Set off**: To begin a journey
- **All the same**: Nevertheless or despite that
- **Make a decision**: US expression for choosing
- **Decision-maker**: Person who makes decisions

Note the irony in the cultural anecdotes — the American tourist's shock at the restaurant bill, the British vs. American terms for the WC. Pay attention to the different cultural products mentioned and their origins. Consider the historical context of immigration and cultural exchange.
