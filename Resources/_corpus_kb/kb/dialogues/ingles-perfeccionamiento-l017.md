## Original

Seventeenth lesson

Statistics

The results of the latest Gallup poll into the health of the nation indicate that 100 % of all people who catch ‘flu die. Read this and you will probably run to the chemist’s in panic to stock your medicine cabinet with the latest patent cures, unless you stop and think it over.

Have you noticed that, today, we are bombarded with statistics from all sides? Politicians, advertisers, the media — even the church — will tell you, uninvited, the latest figures or percentage points. They often use them as a drunk uses a lamp post: for support rather than for illumination.

And we obediently read them in amazement, slap our foreheads and exclaim — Oh, no!, or — Fancy that!
Doctor Johnson must have seen the writing on the wall two hundred years ago when he exclaimed:
— There are lies, then greater lies... then statistics!

Take the case of the Birmingham woman who, in her first two years of marriage, gave birth to two children but avoided having a baby in the third year because she had read in the papers that one person in three in Birmingham ended up before the courts.

This mania for figures is well illustrated in the following extract from a court hearing:
— Then Mrs Ivers, the collision occurred at the junction of Church Road and Bedford Gardens. How far away were you?
— Fifty four yards (54 yds), two feet (2ft) and three inches (3 ins), m’lud.
— You are very precise, madam. Did you measure the distance?
— Yes, m’lud. — And may I ask why?
— Because I knew some idiot would ask me... m’lud.

But, when all is said and done, it is often better to be ignorant of figures.
It was recently calculated that the Lord’s Prayer contains fifty six words, the Ten Commandments has two hundred and ninety seven, The American Declaration of Independence has three hundred — but a recent EEC directive on the import of caramel and caramel products needed twenty six thousand nine hundred and eleven words.

Would anyone care to calculate the percentage of inflation?

## Translation

Decimoséptima lección

Las estadísticas

Los resultados de la última encuesta Gallup sobre la salud de la nación indican que mueren el 100 % de las personas que contraen una gripe. Lea esto y probablemente irá corriendo alarmado a la farmacia para surtir su botiquín con los últimos medicamentos milagrosos, a menos que se detenga a reflexionar.

¿Se ha dado cuenta de que hoy día nos bombardean con estadísticas por todos lados? Los políticos, la publicidad, los medios de comunicación... Suelen utilizarlos del mismo modo que un borracho utiliza un poste del alumbrado: para apoyarse más que para alumbrarse.

y nosotros, obedientemente, los leímos, asombrados, nos golpeamos la frente y exclamamos: ¡Oh, no!, o bien ¡No me diga!
El Dr. Johnson debió haber visto las palabras escritas en la pared hace doscientos años cuando exclamó:
— ¡Existen las mentiras, seguidas de mentiras más grandes... y, al final, las estadísticas!

Tomemos el caso de la mujer de Birmingham que, en sus dos primeros años de casada, dio a luz a dos niños, pero evitó tener un bebé en el tercer año debido a que leyó en los periódicos que una persona de cada tres en Birmingham terminaba ante los tribunales.

Esta manía por las cifras se ilustra muy bien en el extracto siguiente de una audiencia en los tribunales:
— Entonces, señora Ivers, el choque ocurrió en el cruce de Church Road y Bedford Gardens. ¿A qué distancia estaba usted?
— Cincuenta y cuatro yardas (50 m), dos pies (0,6 m) y tres pulgadas (7,6 cm), señor juez.
— Usted es muy precisa, señora. ¿Midió la distancia?
— Sí, señor juez. — ¿Y puedo preguntarle por qué?
— Porque sabía que algún idiota me preguntaría... ¡Señor juez!

Pero, a fin de cuentas, a veces es mejor ignorar las cifras.
Se calculó recientemente que el Padre Nuestro contiene cincuenta y seis palabras; los Diez Mandamientos tienen doscientas noventa y siete. La Declaración de Independencia Americana contiene trescientas — pero una directiva reciente de la CEE sobre la importación de caramelo y los productos derivados del caramelo necesitó veintiséis mil novecientas once palabras.

¿Se preocuparía alguien de calcular el porcentaje de inflación?

## Lesson notes

- **Gallup poll**: Generic term for opinion polls, though *opinion poll* is more accurate. George Gallup (1901–1986) pioneered scientific polling.
- **Chemist**: In GB, means *pharmacist* (US: *drugstore/druggist*). *Go to the chemist’s* = visit a pharmacy.
- **Patent medicine**: Commercial medicine sold under a brand name (*MED-i-sin*). *Pat. pending* = patent applied for.
- **Think over**: Reflect carefully. *Don’t decide now. Think it over for a few days.*
- **For support rather than for illumination**: Metaphor for using statistics to justify positions rather than to inform.
- **Fancy that!**: Exclamation of surprise. *Fancy Michael getting married!* = Imagine that!
- **See the writing on the wall**: Foresee an inevitable outcome (biblical origin: Daniel interpreting Nebuchadnezzar’s vision).
- **Give birth to**: Formal for *have a child*. Also used metaphorically (*Computers have given birth to a new era*).
- **Imperial measurements**: 12 inches = 1 foot (ft), 3 feet = 1 yard (yd). Note *five foot four* (no hyphen) for height.
- **m’lud**: Pronunciation of *My Lord*, addressing a judge (GB: *My Lord*; US: *Your Honor*).
- **Lord’s Prayer/Ten Commandments**: Use singular verbs (*the Prayer contains*, *the Commandments has*).
- **EEC**: European Economic Community, precursor to the EU.

## Exercises

**Sentence practice**
1. If you stop and think it over, his salary is quite low.
2. You can count on my full support for the project.
3. — Car in the garage again? — Yes. Some idiot ran into the back of me at a traffic light.
4. Would anyone care for another drink?
5. Can you buy medicines from a chemist without a prescription in America? No, but we can buy them from the drugstore.

**Fill in the missing words**
1. ¿Quién se imaginaba que Margaret se casaría sin decírselo a nadie? → Fancy ______ ______ married without telling anyone.
2. Una persona de cada cuatro termina en la cárcel. → One person ______ ______ ______ ______ in prison.
3. No puede durar. Está claro como la luz del día. → It can’t last. The ______ ______ ______ ______.
4. ¿Cuándo nació usted? → ______ ______ ______ born?
5. Él debe haber visto lo que iba a pasar. → He ______ ______ ______ what was going to happen.

**Answers**: 1. Margaret getting, 2. in four ends up, 3. writing is on the wall, 4. When were you, 5. must have seen

## Vocabulary highlights

- **Gallup poll**: opinion survey
- **chemist’s**: pharmacy (GB)
- **patent cure**: proprietary medicine
- **lamp post**: street light pole
- **Fancy that!**: expression of surprise
- **writing on the wall**: ominous sign
- **give birth to**: deliver a baby
- **junction**: intersection (roads)
- **yard/foot/inch**: imperial length units
- **m’lud**: abbreviation for *My Lord* (judge)

## Patterns

| Form | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| be bombarded with | overwhelmed by | Bombarded with statistics |
| for A rather than for B | preferring A to B | For support, not illumination |
| see the writing on the wall | foresee trouble | He saw the writing on the wall |
| give birth to | produce (literal/fig.) | Gave birth to twins |
| end up | ultimately be | Ended up in court |

## Agent cues

- **Critical reading**: Question how statistics are used in media/politics.
- **Cultural note**: British vs. American terms (*chemist’s* vs. *drugstore*).
- **Humor**: Churchill’s quip highlights misuse of statistics.
- **Pronunciation**: *54 yds* = fifty-four yards (*yds* = yards).
