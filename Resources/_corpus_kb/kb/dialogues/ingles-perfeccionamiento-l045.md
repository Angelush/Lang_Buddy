## Original

1 Words that connect two ideas are called relatives (by the way, your relatives are your aunts, cousins, etc.).
2 Two such words that people use quite often are which and that.
3 The invention that won the prize was a tilt-rotor aero-engine; and
4 The invention, which won the prize, was developed by the American company Howlett.

5 One's first reaction may be: What's the difference? But look again at these sentences: and pick out the vital information.
6 In the first example, we define the invention (it is the invention that won the prize),
7 whereas in the second example, the fact that the invention won the prize is additional information.

8 The fuel consumption of the car is very high, which worries me.
9 The houses, which belonged to the municipal authorities, were badly in need of repair.
10 The houses that belonged to the authorities were dilapidated; all the others were in a good state of repair.
11 Here is the project that he has been working on for the last seven months.
12 The project, which he has been working on for seven months, will be discussed by the research committee tomorrow.

13 One last point — if the relative is present in a defining clause, we can leave it out if we want.
14 The house I looked at last month is worth another twenty thousand pounds.
15 You cannot do this if the words are separated by two commas: The house, which I bought last year, is now worth £80,000.

## Translation

1 Las palabras que vinculan dos ideas se llaman relativos (a propósito, vuestros parientes (relatives) son vuestros tíos, primos, etc.).
2 Dos de estas palabras que la gente tiene muy presentes son which y that. Ejemplos:
3 El invento que (that) ganó el premio era un motor de avión giratorio; y
4 el invento, el cual (which) ganó el premio, fue perfeccionado por la compañía americana Howlett.

5 La primera reacción de uno puede ser: ¿Qué diferencia hay? Pero mire otra vez estas frases: y elija la información más importante.
6 En el primer ejemplo, definimos el invento (es el invento el que ganó el premio),
7 mientras que en el segundo ejemplo, el hecho de que el invento ganara el premio es una información complementaria.

8 El consumo de combustible del coche es muy alto, lo cual me inquieta.
9 Las casas, que pertenecían a la municipalidad, tenían necesidad urgente de reparación.
10 Las casas que pertenecían a las autoridades estaban estropeadas; las otras estaban en buen estado.
11 Aquí está el proyecto en el que lleva trabajando siete meses.
12 El proyecto, en el que él trabaja desde hace siete meses, se discutirá mañana por el Comité de investigación.

13 Una última cosa — si el relativo está presente en una oración restrictiva, podemos desecharlo si queremos.
14 La casa (que) yo miré el mes pasado vale ahora veinte mil libras más.
15 No puedes hacer esto si las palabras están separadas por dos comas: La casa, que compré el año pasado, vale ahora 80.000 libras.

## Lesson notes
- **Relatives/Relatives**: Note the homonym — *relatives* can mean both connecting words and family members.
- **Motor vs. Engine**: Generally, *engine* is larger (car engine), while *motor* is smaller, but both can mean *motor*. A *tilt-rotor* is a type of aircraft engine combining features of helicopters and airplanes.
- **To tilt**: to slope or incline; to tip over.
- **Repair**: *To repair* = to fix. *To be in a good/bad state of repair* = to be in good/bad condition. Note: not used for people. Alternative: *to be in good/bad condition*.
- **Dilapidated**: in a state of disrepair or ruin.
- **To leave out**: to omit. More natural than the Latinate *to omit*.
- **Defining vs. Non-defining clauses**: Defining clauses (typically with *that*) give essential information about the noun — they define which specific noun is meant. Non-defining clauses (with *which*) give additional, non-essential information. **Rule**: Defining clauses have NO commas; non-defining clauses ARE separated by commas.
- **Comma rule**: If you can remove the relative clause without losing the meaning of the sentence, it's non-defining — use *which* with commas. If you cannot remove it without changing the meaning, it's defining — use *that* without commas.
- **Omitting the relative**: In defining clauses, the relative pronoun can often be omitted if it's the object of the clause: *The house I saw* (but NOT *The house which I saw*).

## Exercises
1. Are you any relation to the author with the same name?
2. The engine tilts backwards to force the aircraft upwards.
3. Take special care when you are overtaking on slippery roads.
4. The thing that really worries me is the maintenance: it costs a fortune to repair.
5. The house was in an excellent state of repair; we were able to move in right away.
6. How many miles do you get to the gallon with that new sports car?

Fill in the missing words:
1. The ship, ......... was registered in Spain, sank near the French coast. (which)
2. The ship .... took my grandfather to America was the Titanic! (that)
3. The house .... | bought last year has doubled in price. (that, or omit)

## Vocabulary highlights
- **Relative**: A word that connects clauses (also: family member)
- **Relatives**: Family members (plural)
- **Invention**: A created device or process
- **Tilt-rotor**: Aircraft combining helicopter and airplane features
- **Aero-engine**: Aircraft engine
- **Repair**: The act of fixing; state of being fixed
- **Dilapidated**: Fallen into disrepair
- **State of repair**: Condition (good or bad)
- **Leave out**: To omit or exclude
- **Defining clause**: Essential relative clause specifying the noun
- **Non-defining clause**: Non-essential relative clause adding extra information
- **Vital**: Essential or crucial
- **Whereas**: Contrasting conjunction (while, although)
- **Motor/Engine**: Both translate to *motor* in Spanish; *engine* typically larger

## Patterns
- **Defining relative clause with *that***: specifies which noun is meant (*The invention **that** won the prize...*)
- **Non-defining relative clause with *which***: adds extra, non-essential information (*The invention, **which** won the prize, was developed by Howlett*)
- **Omission of relative pronoun**: in defining clauses when it's the object (*The house **I saw*** = *The house **that I saw***)

## Agent cues
Focus on distinguishing essential vs. non-essential information. Practice: (1) identifying defining vs. non-defining clauses, (2) correct comma usage, (3) when to use *that* vs. *which*, (4) omitting relative pronouns in defining clauses. Note that *that* cannot be used in non-defining clauses (*The project, that... ❌*). Pay attention to the intonation: pause before and after non-defining clauses.
