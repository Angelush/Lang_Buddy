## Original

1 The verb to have offers an excellent example of English practice, as well as the difference between British and American English. It concerns what is "correct" and what is usual: there are two verbs "to have" — as a main verb and as an auxiliary. Observe how they are used in questions and negatives:

He has a new car.
He doesn't have a new car.
Does he have a new car?

She has a cold.
She doesn't have a cold.
Does she have a cold?

Now as an auxiliary:

He has bought a car.
He hasn't bought a car.
Has he bought a car?

They have seen it.
They haven't seen it.
Have they seen it?

As you can see, there is no difficulty. However, many English speakers confuse the two forms and tend to use it always as an auxiliary, so we would have: *He hasn't a new car*, or *Has he a new car?*

Now pay attention... As forms like *Has he a new car?* are sometimes difficult to understand, we add a word: *got*. THIS WORD HAS NO MEANING OF ITS OWN! It simply signals that we are using the verb, not the auxiliary.

Has he got a new car?
Has she got a cold?

If you analyze it, the verb is *has*; *got* has no meaning. This explains why English speakers can confuse the two forms, and sometimes think *Has she a cold?* is more elegant than *Does she have a cold?*, when in fact the first example is WRONG and the second is CORRECT!

Remember: when *have* is a verb, treat it like any other verb (interrogative and negative with *do/does*). When *have* is an auxiliary, modify with the pronoun.

Curiously, American English is more grammatically strict than British English in this regard, and confuses the two forms much less. So if a Brit asks an American: *Have you got...*, the answer will be: *Yes, I do* (meaning the question should have been *Do you have...*).

2 The use of the definite article is indeed quite complicated, and deserves special attention. In this note, we refer to the main problem: the distinction between defined/undefined:

(1) Most people take holidays in July.
Most people (in general) take holidays in July.

(2) Most of the people that we met were on holiday.
The majority of the people that we met were on holiday.

Do you see the difference? In (1) we talk about people in general, without specifying. In (2), they are the specific people we met. A simple rule: if you can answer the question "which one(s)?" you must add the definite article. *Most of the people we met* (which people? the ones we met).

Now you have the basic rule. Let's apply it referring to Lesson 10, note 2.

3 Shall as an auxiliary for the first person singular to form the future. Here is a good example (/ shall go, you will go, etc.) that was once used in English teaching. The distinction was valid in former times and is found in old books, but contractions have changed everything! Indeed, *I'll* is the contraction of *I shall* or *I will*? Today, *I will* has become the norm.

It used to be taught that *shall* was used for a decisive future or formal promise (you WILL do it, no doubt). This form no longer exists in this sense, except in legal and official language where *shall* implies obligation. In planes: *This curtain shall be closed before take off*. In our example (lesson 9), the prophet tells us that the lion and the lamb shall lie down together.

4 Yes, cricket is part of the practice! There is a belief that it's impossible to explain the rules, but in fact, nothing is simpler! Two teams of eleven players each defend, each in their own way, pitches defined by three wooden wickets. Points are scored by batsmen who run between two marked wickets; when a batsman sends the ball to the boundary of the playing field, he can score between four and six points. The batsman is out when the wicket is hit and falls or when the ball is caught in the air by a player from the opposing team. Normally each team plays two innings in a match; when playing in defense, they are *in*, and their opponents try to put them *out*. Easy, no? *The ins and outs of a problem* are the pros and cons, advantages and disadvantages.

5 When you start to really know a language, you become interested in its familiar language, its street slang. And this is when you must pay the most attention, because using these terms and expressions implies that you belong to a certain linguistic community. Now, for a foreigner, this can be delicate because precisely, he does not belong to this community. Therefore, unless you have an excellent command of the language and pronunciation, you may seem ridiculous or rude. So, if we present in this book a certain number of popular slang words that are more "usual," it is so you can recognize and understand them, NOT to use them. The word *bloody* is often used in spoken British English in place of the adjective to reinforce the meaning of a feeling, or simply to indicate harshness or coarseness: *the bloody match* = the fierce match. The word, like many others, is considered vulgar because of its religious origin (the blood in question was Christ's blood). See also lesson 27.

## Translation

1 El verbo *to have* nos ofrece un excelente ejemplo de la práctica del inglés, así como la diferencia entre el inglés de G.B. y el que se habla en E.U. Se trata de lo que es «correcto» y lo que es usual: en realidad, existen dos verbos *to have* — como verbo propiamente dicho y como auxiliar.

2 La utilización del artículo definido es en efecto bastante complicada, y merece que se le preste una atención especial en el estudio de este libro. En esta nota, vamos a referirnos al problema principal: la distinción entre definido/no definido:

(1) La mayor parte de las gentes toman vacaciones en julio. (en general)
(2) La mayor parte de las gentes que nos hemos encontrado estaban de vacaciones. (específicas)

¿Usted percibe la diferencia? En (1) se habla de las gentes en general, sin precisar. En (2) son las gentes específicas que hemos encontrado. Una regla sencilla: si se puede contestar a la pregunta: ¿el (los) cual (cuales)? es necesario añadir el artículo definido. *Most of the people we met* (¿qué personas? las que nos hemos encontrado).

Ahora usted dispone de la regla básica.

3 *Shall* como auxiliar de la primera persona del singular para formar el futuro. Este es un buen ejemplo que era utilizado antiguasmente en la enseñanza del inglés. La distinción era válida en otros tiempos y se encuentra en ciertos libros antiguos, pero las contracciones lo han cambiado todo! En efecto, ¿*I'll* es la contracción de *I shall* o de *I will*? Hoy en día, *I will* se ha hecho la norma.

4 ¡Sí, el cricket forma parte de la práctica! Existe la creencia que es imposible de explicar el reglamento, sin embargo, ¡no hay nada más simple! Dos equipos compuestos por once jugadores defienden, cada uno a su manera, unos espacios definidos por tres señales de madera en el terreno. Los puntos son marcados por los bateadores, que corren entre las dos marcas señaladas en el terreno; cuando un bateador envía la pelota hasta los límites del terreno de juego puede marcar entre cuatro y seis puntos. El bateador queda eliminado cuando la señal de madera es golpeada y se derrumba o cuando la pelota es atrapada en el aire por un jugador del equipo contrario. Normalmente cada equipo juega dos mangas en un partido; cuando se juega en defensa se dice que está *in*, y sus adversarios tratan de ponerle fuera de combate, es decir *out*. ¡Fácil, no le parece! *The ins and outs of a problem* son los pormenores, las ventajas y desventajas de un problema.

5 Cuando se comienza a conocer bien una lengua, nos interesa estudiar su parte familiar, su jerga callejera. Y es entonces cuando debemos prestar más atención, porque la utilización de estos términos y expresiones implica que se pertenece a cierto grupo o comunidad lingüística. Ahora bien, para un extranjero, esto puede ser muy delicado porque justamente, él no pertenece a dicha comunidad. Por lo tanto, a menos que se tenga un excelente dominio del idioma y la pronunciación, puede parecer ridículo o grosero. Así, si presentamos en este libro un cierto número de palabras del argot popular que son más «usuales», es con el propósito de que las reconozcan y las comprendan y no para que las utilicen. La palabra *bloody* se utiliza a menudo en el inglés que se habla en G.B. en lugar del adjetivo para reforzar el sentido de un sentimiento, en lugar del *very* o simplemente para indicar dureza o grosería: *the bloody match* = el encarnizado partido. La palabra, como muchas otras, es considerada como vulgar a causa de su origen religioso (la sangre en cuestión era la sangre de Cristo).

## Lesson notes

**Verb to have review**:
- As main verb: *He has a car. Does he have a car? He doesn't have a car.*
- As auxiliary: *He has bought a car. Has he bought a car? He hasn't bought a car.*
- UK confusion: *Have you got a car?* (colloquial, *got* = meaningless marker)
- US preference: *Do you have a car?* (grammatically correct)

**Article review**:
- Defined: specific, known reference (*the people we met*)
- Undefined: general, unspecified (*Most people take holidays*)
- Test: Can you answer "which one(s)?" If yes → use *the*

**Shall vs Will**:
- Historical: *shall* = 1st person future, *will* = others
- Modern: *will* for all persons
- Legal/Official: *shall* = obligation (*This curtain shall be closed...*)
- Contractions: *I'll* = *I shall* or *I will* → caused confusion

**Cricket basics**:
- Two teams of 11
- Batsmen score by running between wickets
- 4-6 points for boundary hits
- Out when wicket hit or ball caught
- Two innings per team
- *In* = batting, *out* = fielding

**Slang warning**:
- *Bloody* = intensifier (can be vulgar, religious origin)
- Recognize slang, but use cautiously
- Foreigners may misuse slang inappropriately
- Better to understand than to use until very fluent

## Vocabulary highlights

- **to have (main verb)**: tener (necesita do/does en negativo/interrogativo)
- **to have (auxiliary)**: haber (perfecto: *has bought, have seen*)
- **got**: palabra sin significado, marca el uso de *have* como verbo
- **shall**: futuro (antiguo/legal), hoy *will* es la norma
- **cricket**: críquet (11 jugadores, *wicket*, *bat*, *ball*)
- **ins and outs**: pormenores, detalles
- **bloody**: intensificador (puede ser vulgar)
- **street slang**: jerga callejera
