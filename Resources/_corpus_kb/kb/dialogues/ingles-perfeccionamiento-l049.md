## Original

1 The evolution of the English language over time and the fact that it is spoken by more than 350 million people, have allowed it to free itself from irregularities and difficulties that burden other languages; thus English has become a "simple" language.

2 But this logic made of simplicity does not depart from reality: between 1926 and 1930, the English writer and linguist Charles Kay Ogden established a variant of English, which he baptized Basic English. This new language, whose vocabulary and to a certain extent grammar are derived from English, contained in its basic version only 850 words (600 nouns, 150 adjectives and 100 function words like can, to, if, etc.). Basic English aspired to be the international auxiliary language and, as such, received the support of Winston Churchill and Franklin Roosevelt in 1943. Fortunately, we can say that this "poor relation" aroused little interest in political and official circles, although when we think sometimes that there are too many words in English, we won't take the path of 1984 in the near future.

3 A rose is a rose is a rose... is the title of a poem by Gertrude Stein (who lived for a long time in France and who pronounced the phrase in response to someone who told her that a rose was more beautiful than other flowers). It could be translated as: A rose is a rose is a rose.

4 Now, as for the relative pronouns: as we have already said, and as Lesson 45 illustrates, the language has evolved and the subjunctive has been subtly replaced by other turns of phrase, also correct but which do not always please the purists and those responsible for safeguarding linguistic purity (a thankless task in the Anglophone world!).

5 The form of the present subjunctive is the same as the infinitive without "to" (be, go), etc. and it is used only in the third person singular: Long live the king!
6 The subjunctive is used in set expressions, such as God save the Queen, as it may... (such as it is) or if I were you.
7 It is normally used after verbs like to command, to insist, to order, etc., but as we have seen, it is often replaced by either the infinitive without "to" (which, it must be admitted, is somewhat vicious, because the subjunctive and the infinitive without "to" have exactly the same form!) or by other turns of phrase to avoid the problem altogether: The accountant recommended that the company buy a new machine.

8 So, apart from set expressions, we find little of the subjunctive (and sometimes we don't recognize it when we see it!), and the English unconsciously find ways to avoid it (in a formal style, Americans have less tendency to avoid this mood; see Lesson 46).

9 In particular, the goal of this lesson is to acquire awareness of the extent of English vocabulary and, consequently, to avoid the temptation of "getting by" with words that all look the same.
10 In fact, to get, a lot, and nice are very used words, which people use to avoid looking for a more appropriate word, and everything becomes nice, the ubiquitous adjective that means everything and says nothing. Better than saying: He's a nice man, let us be precise: charming, delightful, entertaining, and generous...

11 At the beginning of your learning, simple words have been very useful to you, but now it's time to evolve.

12 In lesson forty-seven, we saw some very precise words for describing noises and lights. There are many others, which make English a sensual language rather than an intellectual one (and therefore...).

13 We strongly recommend that you develop your vocabulary by reading and taking note of new words, but always within their context.

14 The English and American parliamentary systems are similar (for example, both are bicameral), but taking into account that one is a monarchy and the other a republic, the political modalities are somewhat different.

15 In Great Britain, Parliament (note pronunciation: PAH-li-ment) is composed of two chambers: the House of Commons and the House of Lords. The first has 650 deputies (Members of Parliament or MPs) elected by universal suffrage every five years. The House of Lords is composed of about 1200 peers (nobles), who are either hereditary peers (the noble title passes from generation to generation) or life peers (appointed by the monarch, and their title disappears with them). The peers are therefore not elected by the people. The head of government is the Prime Minister; there is no limit to the term of a Prime Minister. Great Britain does not have a written constitution.

16 From this lesson on, we will mark with an asterisk the words whose spelling differs between British and American English; refer to the appendix on this subject at the end of the book.

## Lesson notes
- **Basic English**: A simplified version of English created by Charles Kay Ogden and I.A. Richards in the 1920s-30s. It uses a limited vocabulary of 850 words (600 nouns, 150 adjectives, 100 function words). It was intended as an international auxiliary language.
- **Ogden, Charles Kay (1889-1957)**: English writer and linguist who created Basic English with I.A. Richards.
- **Churchill, Winston (1874-1965)**: British Prime Minister who supported Basic English as a means of international communication.
- **Roosevelt, Franklin D. (1882-1945)**: US President who supported Basic English in 1943.
- **Gertrude Stein (1874-1946)**: American writer who lived in France. Her phrase *A rose is a rose is a rose* emphasizes that things are what they are — a rose's essence doesn't change regardless of description. It was her response to someone who said a rose was more beautiful than other flowers.
- **Parliament**: The supreme legislative body in the United Kingdom, composed of the House of Commons and the House of Lords. Note pronunciation: /ˈpɑː.lɪ.mənt/ (PAH-li-ment), not /ˈpær.lə.mənt/.
- **House of Commons**: The lower, elected chamber of the UK Parliament. Members are called MPs (Members of Parliament).
- **House of Lords**: The upper, unelected chamber of the UK Parliament. Members are called peers.
- **MP (Member of Parliament)**: An elected representative in the House of Commons.
- **Peer**: A member of the British nobility. *Hereditary peer* = title passed down through generations. *Life peer* = appointed for life by the monarch; title dies with them.
- **Prime Minister**: The head of government in the UK. There is no term limit.
- **Bicameral**: Having two legislative chambers (House of Commons + House of Lords in UK; House of Representatives + Senate in US).
- **Constitution**: The fundamental principles or established precedents that constitute the legal basis of a polity. The UK does not have a single written constitution document.
- **Asterisk (*)**: A symbol (*) used to mark text, often for footnotes or special attention. In this course, it will indicate words with different spelling in British and American English.

- **Subjunctive review**: The subjunctive mood in English is used for hypothetical, unreal, or uncertain actions. It has largely disappeared from everyday use, replaced by other constructions. Modern usage:
  - Set expressions: *God save the Queen*, *Long live the King!*
  - After certain verbs: *demand*, *insist*, *order*, *request*, *suggest* (though often replaced by *should* or the infinitive)
  - Form: same as the infinitive without *to* for all persons (except 3rd person singular in present tense: *he be* instead of *he is*)
  - Example: *I insist that he **tell** the truth* (subjunctive) vs. *I insist that he **tells** the truth* (informal, more common)

- **Relative clauses review**: 
  - **Defining (restrictive)**: gives essential information — uses *that* (or omission), NO commas. *The book **that I read** was good.*
  - **Non-defining (non-restrictive)**: gives extra information — uses *which*, WITH commas. *The book, **which I read**, was good.*
  - Test: if you can remove the clause without changing the meaning, it's non-defining (use *which* + commas). If you cannot, it's defining (use *that* or omit + no commas).

- **Purity**: The quality of being pure, especially in language

- **Task**: A piece of work to be done

## Agent cues
Focus on consolidating your understanding of key grammatical concepts. Practice: (1) identifying and creating defining vs. non-defining relative clauses, (2) recognizing and using the subjunctive mood appropriately, (3) expanding vocabulary beyond generic words like *nice* or *get*, (4) distinguishing British vs. American English spelling (note asterisk-marked words in future lessons). Pay special attention to the context in which new vocabulary is introduced. Develop awareness of how English has evolved and how it continues to change. Note the historical context of attempts to simplify English (Basic English) and their limited success.
