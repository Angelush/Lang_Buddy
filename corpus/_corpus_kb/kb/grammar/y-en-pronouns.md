## Rule

In French, the pronouns **y** and **en** are used to replace phrases introduced by prepositions to avoid repetition and streamline the sentence.

- **y** replaces a group introduced by the preposition **à** (or other locative prepositions like *dans*, *sur*, *sous*, except *de*). It often refers to a place (locative) or an abstract thing/idea.
- **en** replaces a group introduced by the preposition **de**. It is used for partitives (quantity), origins, or things/ideas introduced by *de*.

## Examples

Verbatim examples from [[sources/french-en-et-y-exos]]:

- Tu peux aller à l’université en autobus. → **Tu peux y aller en autobus.**
- J’arrive au travail vers dix heures. → **J’y arrive vers dix heures.**
- Pierre arrive du stade olympique à l’instant. → **Pierre en arrive à l’instant.**
- Il se sert de son ordinateur pour faire ses devoirs. → **Il s’en sert pour faire ses devoirs.**

## Common errors

- **Confusion with people**: [SPECULATIVE] Learners often try to use *y* or *en* to replace people (e.g., *Je parle à Pierre* -> *J'y parle* is incorrect; it should be *Je lui parle*).
- **Misuse of 'en' for locations**: [SPECULATIVE] English speakers might use *en* thinking of "in", whereas *y* is the correct locative pronoun for "there" (except when the location is introduced by *de*).

## When the agent should correct vs. let it slide

The agent should always correct the misuse of *y* and *en* as they are fundamental to natural-sounding French. However, in the very early stages of oral practice, if the learner uses the full noun phrase instead of the pronoun (e.g., *Je vais à l'école* instead of *J'y vais*), the agent should model the pronominal version in the next turn rather than interrupting with a formal correction.
