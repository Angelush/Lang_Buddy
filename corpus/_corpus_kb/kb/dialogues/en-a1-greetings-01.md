## Dialogue

1 — Hi! Are you Sara? — Yes, I am.
2 — I'm Tom. Nice to meet you.
3 — Nice to meet you too.
4 — Are you new here? — Yes, it's my first day.
5 — Where are you from? — I'm from Canada. And you?
6 — I'm from Spain. I'm from Madrid.
7 — Is this your phone? — No, it isn't. It's Sara's phone.
8 — Oh, sorry! Thank you.

## Pronunciation

- *Nice to meet you* — /naɪs tə ˈmiːt juː/ (the **t** links to *you*: "mee-tchu").
- *Are you* — /ɑːr juː/, often reduced to "er-you".
- *Where are you from?* — /ˌwɛər ə ju ˈfrɒm/ (stress on **from**).
- Contractions: *I'm* /aɪm/, *it's* /ɪts/, *isn't* /ˈɪz.ənt/.

## Translation

1 — ¡Hola! ¿Eres Sara? — Sí, (lo) soy.
2 — Soy Tom. Encantado.
3 — Encantada también.
4 — ¿Eres nueva aquí? — Sí, es mi primer día.
5 — ¿De dónde eres? — Soy de Canadá. ¿Y tú?
6 — Soy de España. Soy de Madrid.
7 — ¿Es este tu teléfono? — No, no lo es. Es el teléfono de Sara.
8 — ¡Ah, perdón! Gracias.

## Notes

- **(1)** *Nice to meet you* is the fixed greeting when you meet someone for the
  first time. The natural reply is *Nice to meet you too*. English has no
  masculine/feminine ending here, unlike Spanish *encantado/encantada*.
- **(2)** Contractions are the spoken norm: *I am → I'm*, *it is → it's*,
  *it is not → it isn't*. The full forms sound stiff in conversation.
- **(3)** *Where are you from?* asks origin; answer with *I'm from + place*.
  Note the word order: the question word *where* comes first, then *are you*.
- **(4)** Possession uses **'s**: *Sara's phone* = "el teléfono de Sara". English
  puts the owner first, the opposite of Spanish.

## Exercises

1. Complete with the contraction: "I .. Tom." / "It .. my first day."
2. Make it negative: "It is my phone." → "It .. my phone."
3. Ask the question: "______ are you from?" — "I'm from Mexico."
4. Reorder: from / where / you / are → ?

**Answer key:** 1 'm / 's. 2 isn't. 3 Where. 4 Where are you from?

## Agent cues

Strong Anticipation gaps: line 1 (*Yes, I __*), line 5 (*I'm from ___*), line 7 (the
*it isn't* contraction). Model the contractions first, then pull them. Correct
gently by recasting if the learner drops the subject *it* ("isn't mine" →
"it isn't mine"). Register is friendly-neutral; keep pace very slow with clear
pauses after each question.
