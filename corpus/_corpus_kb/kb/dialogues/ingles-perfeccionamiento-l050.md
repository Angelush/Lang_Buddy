## Original

1 This land is your land, this land is my land, from California to the New York islands...
2 So sang Woody Guthrie, and millions of people both American and non-American echo his sentiments about this most fascinating of countries.
3 Its influence is felt throughout the world, but America is a land of such diversity and of so many contrasts, that it is impossible to think of the United States as a single entity.

4 From the golden beaches of California, through the sun-baked deserts of Arizona,
5 across the vast, flat plains of the Middle West, to the covered bridges of Vermont
6 and the eclectic atmosphere and towering skyscrapers of New York:
7 a journey across America is a journey through a bewildering array
8 of scenery and cultures that leave the traveler with a host of impressions and opinions.

9 This lesson is not intended to be a travelogue, so let's stop for a moment
10 at a place where so many people have seen their first — and sometimes last —
11 glimpse of the land that opened its gates: Ellis Island in New York Sound.

12 Popularly known as the Isle of Tears, Ellis Island was the processing center
13 for newly arrived immigrants from the Old World.

14 From 1892 to 1943, would-be new Americans had to pass through this place
15 where the fateful decision was made: could they or could they not
16 enter the country and start a new life?
17 Age, infirmity, a criminal record — any one of these was enough to send
18 immigrants back to where they had come from.
19 Others, more fortunate, were allowed to enter, but often with a new name
20 because the immigration officials were unable to read or to pronounce the foreign names.
21 All this went on under the gaze of the symbol of the United States: the Statue of Liberty Guiding the World.
22 Today's traveler jets into New York's JFK airport without even noticing the island;
23 but it is worth taking the time to visit the Isle of Tears, and to imagine yourself
24 arriving there one hundred years ago
25 in the hope of starting afresh in the New World.
26 It is a good way to begin to understand the making of the United States of America.

## Lesson notes
- **This Land Is Your Land**: A famous folk song by **Woody Guthrie** (1912-1967), written in 1940. It has become an unofficial national anthem of the United States, celebrating the beauty and diversity of the American landscape. The song states that the land belongs to all people.
- **Woody Guthrie**: American folk singer and songwriter, known as the "Dust Bowl Troubadour." His songs often addressed social issues and the struggles of common people during the Great Depression.
- **Travelogue**: A piece of writing, a talk, or a film about travel, often illustrated. From *travel* + *monologue*. The lesson is not a travelogue but uses travel as a framework.
- **Glimpse**: A momentary or partial view. *To catch a glimpse of* = to see briefly. *He caught a glimpse of the celebrity before she disappeared.*
- **Ellis Island**: An island in New York Harbor that served as the main immigration station for the United States from **1892 to 1943**. Approximately 12 million immigrants passed through Ellis Island during this period.
- **Isle of Tears**: The nickname for Ellis Island, referring to both the tears of joy (at being admitted to America) and tears of sorrow (at being separated from family or denied entry).
- **Old World**: Europe, Asia, and Africa — the parts of the world known to Europeans before the discovery of the Americas. The Americas are the *New World*.
- **Fateful decision**: A decision with serious or far-reaching consequences. The decision at Ellis Island determined whether immigrants could enter the US.
- **Age/Infirmity/Criminal record**: Grounds for denying entry to immigrants at Ellis Island. Even one of these could result in deportation.
- **To start afresh/start anew**: To begin again from the beginning, often with a new opportunity. *After the war, many people moved to start afresh.*
- **Statue of Liberty**: Officially *Liberty Enlightening the World* (*La Liberté éclairant le monde* in French). A colossal copper statue on Liberty Island in New York Harbor, a gift from France to the United States, dedicated on October 28, 1886. It is a symbol of freedom and democracy, and a welcoming sight for immigrants arriving in New York.
- **JFK Airport**: John F. Kennedy International Airport in Queens, New York City. One of the busiest airports in the world, opened in 1948.
- **To make a decision (US) / to take a decision (UK)**: Different expressions for the same action. *Decision-maker* is used in both dialects.
- **Decision-maker**: A person who makes decisions, especially important ones.
- **Middle West/Midwest**: The north-central region of the United States, comprising states like Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Kansas, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, Nebraska, North Dakota, Ohio, South Dakota, and Wisconsin. Known for its farming and traditional values. Nicknamed *America's breadbasket* due to its agricultural productivity.
- **Covered bridges**: Bridges with a roof and enclosed sides, typical of rural New England. Originally built to protect the bridge deck from weather.
- **Vermont**: A state in the New England region of the northeastern United States. Known for its natural beauty, maple syrup, and progressive politics.
- **Skyscrapers**: Very tall buildings, especially for offices or apartments. New York City is famous for its skyscrapers.
- **Bewildering array**: A confusing or overwhelming variety. *Bewildering* = confusing, *array* = an impressive display or range.
- **Scenery**: The natural features of a landscape considered in terms of their appearance.
- **Host of**: A large number of. *A host of problems* = many problems.
- **To open its gates**: To allow entry. *The country opened its gates to immigrants.*
- **Would-be**: Aspiring or intended. *Would-be actors* = people who want to be actors. *Would-be new Americans* = people who wanted to become Americans.
- **To be unable to**: To not have the ability to. *He was unable to read the name.* Note: *unable to* + infinitive.
- **Under the gaze of**: Under the watchful eye of. *The children played under the gaze of their teacher.*
- **Some...!**: Used ironically in exclamations. *Some holiday!* = ¡Vaya vacaciones! *Some friend you are!* = ¡Vaya amigo que eres!

## Patterns
- **Travelogue description**: Using vivid language to describe geographical features and cultural phenomena
- **Geographical description**: Precise vocabulary for landscapes (beaches, deserts, plains, bridges, skyscrapers)
- **Historical narrative**: Telling the story of Ellis Island and American immigration
- **Superlative construction**: *this most fascinating of countries* for emphasis
- **Cultural diversity description**: Highlighting the variety of American landscapes and cultures

## Agent cues
Focus on the geographical and cultural diversity of the United States. Practice: (1) describing different types of landscapes using precise vocabulary, (2) narrating historical events (Ellis Island immigration process), (3) using superlative constructions for emphasis, (4) understanding the emotional journey of immigrants. Note the contrast between the romanticized view of America (*America the Beautiful*) and the harsh realities of immigration (*Isle of Tears*). Pay attention to the symbolic importance of the Statue of Liberty and Ellis Island in American history and identity. Consider the emotional significance of starting a new life in a foreign country.
