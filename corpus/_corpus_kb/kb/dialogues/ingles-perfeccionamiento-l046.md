## Original

1 A crowd had gathered outside Buckingham Palace, waiting for the Queen to appear on the balcony.
2 When she finally came out, the crowd shouted — God save the Queen!
3 Perplexed, a young German student took his grammar book from his pocket, opened it and said aloud:
4 — That's wrong! — You don't like the Queen?, came the ominous reply.

5 — No, it's not that. Look, you say: the lifeguard saves the drowning man, right?
6 — Yes, replied the crowd.
7 — Listen, son, the twenty thousand people here can't be wrong. Don't try to be too clever.
8 — If I were you, I wouldn't worry, added an old man.
9 — Ah! There you go again!, cried the German. You say: when I was in London, but it should be "were you". Help! I'm going mad. Can someone help me?
10 If anyone here is a grammar teacher, insist that he tells me the answer.
11 — That should be "insist that he tell me", actually, said an elegantly dressed gentleman.
12 — That's the last straw! Explain why. — Subjunctive, old fellow, came the reply.
13 Other languages have it, though. It seems too affected, apparently. However, other languages use it.
14 You see, English does like things simple, and the subjunctive is a difficult subject matter.
15 First it's got the same form as the infinitive, without the to, of course.
16 That means that you only realize the subjunctive in the third person singular, you see.
17 You also find it with verbs like to demand, to ask, or to request.
18 When a person says: "I insist that...", "He demanded that..." —
19 we put a subjunctive after it: He insists that he tell me, or, The judge insisted that the witness answer the question.

20 Are you still with me? Before our perplexed student could answer, another voice interrupted:
21 — Don't listen to that old fuddy-duddy!
22 A crowd of curious onlookers had gathered to listen to the impromptu lesson.
23 — What this bloke says is all well and good in theory, but no one talks like that nowadays.
24 We find ways around the subjunctive. Here, take the examples that he gave you.
25 "I insist that he tells me" is OK, and "The judge insisted that the witness should answer the question"
26 sounds much less pompous than the other sentence.
27 — I object, you said the older man, I insist that...

## Translation

2 Cuando salió al fin, la muchedumbre clamó — ¡Dios salve a la Reina!
3 Perplejo, un joven estudiante alemán sacó su libro de gramática del bolsillo, lo abrió y dijo en alta voz:
4 — ¡Eso es falso! — ¿No te agrada la Reina?, le preguntó alguien con tono amenazador.

5 — No, no es eso. Mira, tú dices: el socorrista salva al nadador que se ahoga, ¿verdad?
6 — Sí, respondió la multitud.
7 — Escucha, hijo, las veinte mil personas que se encuentran aquí no pueden estar equivocadas. No intentes pasarte de listo.
8 — Si yo estuviera en tu lugar, no me preocuparía, añadió un anciano.
9 — ¡Ah! Vuelta a lo mismo, crió el alemán. Tú dices: cuando yo estaba en Londres, pero debería ser "estuvieras tú". ¡Socorro! Me estoy volviendo loco. ¿Puede alguien ayudarme?
10 Si hay alguien aquí que sea profesor de gramática, insisto en que él me diga la respuesta.
11 — Eso debería ser "insisto en que él me diga", en realidad, dijo un hombre elegantemente vestido.
12 — ¡Eso es el colmo! Explique por qué. — El subjuntivo, amigo, fue la respuesta.
13 Otros idiomas lo tienen, sin embargo. Parece demasiado afectado, al parecer. Sin embargo, otros idiomas lo utilizan.
14 Usted ve, el inglés tiende verdaderamente a simplificar las cosas, y el subjuntivo es un tema difícil.
15 Primero tiene la misma forma que el infinitivo, sin el "to", por supuesto.
16 Eso significa que uno solo se da cuenta del subjuntivo en la tercera persona del singular, ¿ves?
17 También lo encuentra con verbos como exigir, preguntar o solicitar.
18 Cuando una persona dice: "Insisto en que...", "Él exigió que..." —
19 colocamos el subjuntivo después: Insisto en que él me diga, o, El juez insistió en que el testigo respondiera a la pregunta.

20 ¿Sigues entendiendo? Antes que nuestro perplejo estudiante pudiera responder, otra voz intervino:
21 — No escuches a ese viejo fósil.

23 — Lo que dice este tipo está muy bien en teoría, pero nadie habla así en la actualidad.
24 Nosotros encontramos formas de esquivar el subjuntivo. Aquí, toma los ejemplos que ese viejo fósil acaba de darle.
25 "Yo insisto en que él me diga" (sin subjuntivo) es perfectamente aceptable, y "el juez insistió en que el testigo debería responder a la pregunta" (sin subjuntivo)

## Lesson notes
- **Buckingham Palace**: Official London residence of the British monarch. Related: *10 Downing Street* (Prime Minister's residence), *11 Downing Street* (Chancellor of the Exchequer), *The White House* (US President).
- **Ominous**: threatening or foreboding evil. "An ominous silence" = a threatening silence. "The sky looks ominous" = the sky looks threatening.
- **Drowning**: present participle of *to drown* (to die from suffocation in water). "A drowning man" = un hombre que se ahoga.
- **To butt in**: to interrupt or interfere. "Don't butt into our conversation." Note: *butt in* (no hyphen) is the phrasal verb.
- **Fuddy-duddy**: slang for an old-fashioned, stubborn person. "Don't listen to that old fuddy-duddy."
- **Bloke**: British slang for a man or guy. American equivalent: *guy*.
- **Crazy**: can mean insane (*He's crazy*) or very enthusiastic (*I'm crazy about you*).
- **Craze**: a fad or fashion. "Pink hair seems to be the latest craze."
- **Actually**: often used as a filler word meaning "in fact" or "really". Not to be confused with *actualmente* (currently). It's a common discourse marker.
- **Subjunctive in English**: The subjunctive mood expresses hypothetical, unreal, or uncertain actions. In modern English, it's most visible in: (a) set expressions (*God save the Queen*, *Long live the King!*), (b) after verbs like *demand*, *insist*, *request*, *suggest*. Form: base verb form (same as infinitive without *to*) for all persons — *I insist that he **be** there*, *She demanded that he **tell** the truth*.
- **Demand/Ask/Request**: *To demand* implies strong insistence. *To ask* is neutral. *To request* is more formal and polite. *Are requested not to smoke* = Se ruega no fumar.
- **The last straw**: the final problem that makes a situation unbearable. From "the last straw that breaks the camel's back."
- **On purpose**: intentionally. "I didn't break it on purpose."
- **Does for emphasis**: *I **do** like it* = Me gusta **mucho**.

## Patterns
- **Subjunctive after verbs of demanding**: *insist that he tell me*, *demand that he be there*
- **Conditional**: *If I were you, I wouldn't worry*
- **Use of *actually* as discourse marker**: for emphasis or correction
- **Colloquial expressions**: *the last straw*, *on purpose*
- **Formal vs. informal language**: *insist that he tell* (formal) vs. *insist that he tells* (informal)

## Agent cues
Focus on the use of the subjunctive mood in English. Practice: (1) subjunctive in set expressions (*God save the Queen*), (2) subjunctive after *insist*, *demand*, *request*, (3) conditional *If I were you*. Note how native speakers often avoid the subjunctive in favor of less formal constructions (*I insist that he tells me* instead of *I insist that he tell me*). Pay attention to the cultural context — the British crowd's reaction to the German student's grammatical pedantry. Listen for the difference between formal and informal registers.
