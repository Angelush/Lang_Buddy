## Original

Nineteenth lesson

No news is good news (Part one)

Teacher: — Is the word ‘trousers’ singular or plural? Student: — Singular at the top and plural at the bottom.
As in the title, some words appear to be plural but are not, while others are both singular and plural.
But don’t worry. We are not going to make a mountain out of a molehill. Just listen to the following sentences:

— What time is the news on? — At nine, but you don’t want to watch it, do you?
— Why not? — It’s either disasters or politics.
— Disasters are tragic, politics is boring — and I’m sure the information is misleading.

Economics is so complex that no one really understands all that waffle about inflation. And as for statistics!
Twenty percent increase here, eight percent decrease there, one in three out of work...

— Hold on. Statistics is a very precise science. So is physics and so is mathematics — but not everyone understands them.
— Oh come on, let me turn on the telly. There’s just one item I want to watch. — What?
— The billiards match... No, don’t laugh. At least billiards is non-violent. What’s so funny?
— You’ve been arguing for so long that the news is over.

## Translation

Decimonovena lección

Las malas noticias son las primeras (Primera parte)

Profesor: — ¿La palabra «trousers» es singular o plural? Estudiante: — Singular arriba y plural abajo.
Como en el título, ciertas palabras parecen estar en plural pero no lo están, mientras que otras son tanto singular como plural.
Pero no se inquiete. No vamos a hacer una montaña de un grano de arena. Simplemente escuche las frases siguientes:

— ¿A qué hora son las noticias? — A las nueve, pero no quieres verlas, ¿no es cierto?
— ¿Por qué no? — Son sobre desastres o sobre política.
— Los desastres son trágicos; la política es aburrida — y estoy seguro de que la información es errónea.

La economía es tan compleja que nadie comprende realmente todo ese camelo acerca de la inflación. ¡Y en cuanto a las estadísticas!
Veinte por ciento de aumento aquí, ocho por ciento de disminución allí, uno de cada tres sin trabajo...

— Espere... La estadística es una ciencia muy precisa. Así como la física y la matemática — pero no todo el mundo las comprende.
— Oh, vamos, encendamos la tele. Hay una información que deseo ver. — ¿Cuál?
— El partido de billar... No, no te rías. Por lo menos los billares no son violentos. ¿Qué te hace tanta gracia?
— Has estado discutiendo tanto tiempo que las informaciones se han terminado.

## Lesson notes

- **No news is good news**: Proverb meaning if no bad news has arrived, all is well. *News* is always singular: *The news is bad*, *a piece of news* (rare).
- **Trousers**: Plural noun. Use *a pair of trousers* for one item, but *my trousers are torn* is acceptable (treated as singular in meaning).
- **Make a mountain out of a molehill**: Exaggerate a minor problem. *Mole* = mole (animal); *molehill* = small mound of earth.
- **What time is the news on?**: TV/radio schedules use *on*: *What time is the film on?* (vs. *What time is the match?*).
- **Politics/economics/statistics/mathematics/physics**: All take singular verbs. Abbreviated as *maths* (GB) or *math* (US).
- **Waffle**: In GB, *waffle* (verb) = talk at length without saying anything meaningful. *It’s a load of waffle* = nonsense.
- **Telly**: Colloquial for television (GB; also *the box*). US: *the tube* (from *boob tube* = idiot box).
- **Item**: Rarely used for a single news story; prefer *a news item* only if necessary.
- **Billiards**: Non-violent sport. *Non-* prefix requires hyphen before vowels (*non-violent*, *non-EU*).
- **Be over**: *The news is over* = finished. *It’s all over between us* = relationship ended.

## Exercises

1. Don’t make a mountain out of a molehill. It’s really not important.
2. Politics is a load of waffle.
3. I was misled by the sign: I thought it meant ‘One Way’.
4. He’s as blind as a bat.
5. (On the phone) — He’s sitting opposite me. Hold on, I’ll pass him over.
6. The party was over by the time we arrived.

**Fill in the missing words**
1. La política y la economía forman parte de vuestro programa. → Politics ______ economics ______ part of your course.
2. Usted no apagará la televisión antes de que termine, ¿no es cierto? → You ______ turn off the television before the end, ______?
3. Todos sus muebles proceden del rastro. → Your ______ ______ from the flea-market.
4. ¿A qué hora proyectan la película? → ______ ______ the film ______?
5. Las malas noticias son las primeras. → ______ ______ ______ ______. 

**Answers**: 1. and ... are, 2. won’t ... will you, 3. furniture comes, 4. What time ... on, 5. No news is good news

## Vocabulary highlights

- **trousers**: pants (plural noun)
- **news**: information about recent events (singular)
- **disaster**: catastrophe
- **politics**: art/science of government (singular)
- **economics**: study of economy (singular)
- **waffle**: meaningless talk
- **inflation**: rise in prices
- **telly**: television (colloquial)
- **billiards**: cue sport
- **item**: single piece of news
- **physics**: study of matter/energy (singular)
- **mathematics**: study of numbers (singular)

## Agent cues

- **Grammar focus**: Identify uncountable nouns and their singular verb agreement.
- **Cultural note**: British vs. American terms (*telly* vs. *TV*, *maths* vs. *math*).
- **Media literacy**: Recognize biased language (*waffle*, *misleading*).
- **Proverb practice**: Use *No news is good news* in context.
