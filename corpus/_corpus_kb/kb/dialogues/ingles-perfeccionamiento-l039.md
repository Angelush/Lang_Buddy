## Original
1 Our subject — let’s call him Mr Newman — is a Eurobond dealer who works on the 130th floor of an office building on Wall Street. (2) (3)

He is awakened at 6.00 a.m. by his electronic alarm, and claps his hands to turn on the television.

The dish antenna on his roof brings him direct satellite pictures of what is happening around the world. (4)

After showering, he breakfasts on yogurt and kiwis, dons his roller skates and walkman and skates in to the office. (5)

On arrival, he sits on his ergonomic stool, turns on the VDU of his computer terminal and consults prices in Tokyo and Hong Kong: (6)

The global stockmarket never closes! (7)

10 This morning, he sells futures and options, buying stock in London and selling it in Chicago within seconds. (8)

He only answers the phone when the video unit shows him that an important customer is on the line.

At lunchtime, he walks one block down to the automatic restaurant. There, he buys a steak from a vending machine

and cooks it in the microwave oven at his stand-up table. After a dessert of vitamin pills (9)

he walks across the street to the shopping mall. Here, he buys a videocassette for the evening.

He inserts his credit card into the cash register and a green light comes on: his account is in credit, and the sum is debited immediately. (10)

Mr Newman returns to his office. He checks in his mailbox to see if there are any messages,

and then checks the bulletin board for any bargains that may be on offer. (11)

The afternoon is calm, and at five o’clock, he leaves the office. On his way home, he stops off at his health club (12)

for a game of squash and a jacuzzi; he then skates the ten blocks back to his apartment building.

This evening, Mr Newman is taking his third wife, Judy, to the airport, so he takes his car from the garage,

checks the route on the car’s mapping unit, and the state of the traffic on the on-board computer, and sets off.

He picks his wife up from her office downtown and soon they are speeding out of Manhattan. (13)

He puts his wife on the plane to Denver and heads back towards New York.

Unfortunately, he hasn’t checked the traffic reports, and it takes him over three hours to get home.

He parks his car and takes the elevator up to his apartment. Before going into the kitchen to fix dinner,

he checks the electronic mailbox on his personal computer. There is a message from Judy:

“Arrived safely; thanks for the ride. Love. Judy”

Our time traveller might just recognize, with a wry smile, that not everything changes! (14)

## Lesson notes
- **Big Brother is watching you**: famous slogan from George Orwell’s novel *1984*, warning that society watches its citizens. Note: *1984* lesson appears later (Lesson 41).
- **Eurobond**: a bond = link, tie; commercial: Eurobonds are securities that can be bought/sold outside the country of the currency in which they were issued.
- **Wall Street**: main street of NYC business district; by extension, the American financial establishment. British equivalent = *The City* (London).
- **Dish antenna**: for English speakers, this device looks like a concave dish, hence the name. Note: *antenna* (US) / *aerial* (GB).
- **To don**: literary verb = to put on. Not used in everyday conversation.
- **VDU**: Visual Display Unit = computer monitor. Another acronym: *CPU* = Central Processing Unit.
- **Global stockmarket**: possibility of small stock market transactions at any time of day/night thanks to IT, accessing open exchanges in different time zones.
- **Futures/Options**: speculation forms allowing buying/selling products without having the total sum available. Developed greatly in the 80s. **Stock** = securities (bonds, shares).
- **Microwave oven**: gave rise to verb *to microwave*. *He took a TV dinner from the freezer and microwaved it*. TV dinner = meal served on tray allowing eating without getting up or looking away from TV.
- **EFTPOS**: Electronic Fund Transfer at Point Of Sale = instant bank credit system.
- **Electronic mailbox and bulletin board**: computerized mailbox and notice board.
- **To stop off**: to make a stop, halt on the way. *Stop off on your way home and have a drink with us* = Haga un alto de vuelta a casa para beber una copa con nosotros.
- **Speed**: velocity. *To speed* = go quickly (literary: *The police car sped to the scene of the accident* — irregular). More prosaic = exceed speed limit. *They fined him $60 for speeding* = Le multaron con $60 por exceso de velocidad. In drug slang, *speed* = amphetamines.
- **Wry**: (pron: rai) literally twisted, idiotic. Used figuratively for an ironic, somewhat foolish smile or grimace. Original sense in *awry* (pron: arai) = askew. *He came in with his hat awry on his head* = Él entró con el sombrero atravesado sobre su cabeza.
