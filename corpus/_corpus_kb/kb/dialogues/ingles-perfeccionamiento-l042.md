## Translation
1. We have insisted throughout our course on the importance of the need for tonic accent (stress): however there are a number of words in which the stress changes depending on whether they are used as a verb or as a noun or adjective. Although unknown, this phenomenon is mastered very rapidly and these words are not numerous (we will see again our famous *to get*, but this verb does not have its own meaning, it replaces a precise grammatical function: that of putting a pronoun in the reflexive (or reciprocal) form. We already know, in truth, *to lose* and its reflexive form *to lose oneself*. But this form is a bit “Latin” (and therefore heavy). The most informal form consists of using the past participle of the verb and adding *to*.

For example:

First observe noun/verb pairs where the stress is on the first syllable if the word is a noun and on the second syllable if it is a verb:

an export but to export
an import but to import
a present but to present
a record but to record

Other pairs of words with variable pronunciation are:
the content but he is content (noun/adjective vs adjective)
a perfect day but to perfect (adjective vs verb)

Add a syllable to a word to change its grammatical function:
to advertize but an advertisement
to photograph but a photographer
a desert but to desert

Another category: where the stress changes completely:
to realize but a realization

or again:
They stopped the car by the side of the road = They stopped the car at the roadside
They got stopped by the police = They were stopped by the police

family / familiar
police / policy

“Once again, these are not so much rules as practice.

2. It’s clear that a lack of tonic accent is also a grammatical mistake (one hears a noun instead of a verb, etc.). Observe our examples again and review the recording of Lesson 37 two or three times in order to better capture these differences.

3. We have already seen the problems posed by the fact that English nouns have no gender. Thus, in Spanish, the sentence: *El hotel manager busca un nuevo cocinero; deberá ser muy calificado* is ambiguous, while in English the sentence: *The hotel manager is looking for a new cook; he (or she) must be well qualified* leaves no room for doubt. The same precision is found in advertisements, all the more so since all discrimination is prohibited between men and women in the case of the majority of positions. We only look for a word when comprehension prevents us from appreciating the meaning of the text.

English and American legislation require employers to prioritize ethnic minorities or the disabled (with equal qualifications) for the same job: we find the expression *We are an equal opportunity employer* at the bottom of small ads (We do not practice any kind of discrimination). See also Lesson 10, note 1.

4. *To enjoy* is one of those purely English words for which there is no exact translation. The sense of the verb is stronger than *to love*, it covers the notion of feeling at ease doing something.

## Lesson notes
- **Stress patterns**: English word stress can indicate grammatical function. For many words, stress shifts between noun and verb forms (e.g., **RE-cord** (noun) vs **re-CORD** (verb)). This is a key pronunciation feature for C1 learners.
- **Common noun-verb stress pairs**: export/import, present, record, content, perfect, advertize/advertisement, photograph/photographer, desert, realize/realization.
- **Homographs with different stress**: police (noun) / policy, family / familiar.
- **Phrasal verbs with *get***: The verb *to get* often loses its literal meaning and serves grammatical functions, especially in reflexive constructions (e.g., *get dressed*, *get lost*). This is informal compared to Latinate alternatives.
- **Gender in English**: English nouns lack grammatical gender, making sentences like *The hotel manager is looking for a new cook; he (or she) must be well qualified* unambiguous. Spanish equivalents often require context or clarification.
- **Equal opportunity**: Legislation in English-speaking countries prohibits discrimination. Common phrase: *We are an equal opportunity employer* (no discrimination practiced).
- **To enjoy**: A uniquely English verb encompassing *like*, *love*, and the notion of taking pleasure in an activity. Context is key for translation: *I really enjoyed my meal* (Me encantó la comida), *Did you enjoy the film?* (¿Te gustó la película?), *I hope you enjoyed yourselves* (Espero que lo pasarais bien).

## Patterns
- stress shifts between noun and verb forms
- adding syllables to change grammatical function
- complete stress shifts (to realize / a realization)
- to get + past participle for reflexive (informal)
- he (or she) for gender-neutral reference
- We are an equal opportunity employer
