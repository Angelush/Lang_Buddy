## Original

1 We see Henderson Dores walking up Park Avenue in New York — he is late, he is thinking; and he
2 brings his sabres in a thin bag slung over his right shoulder,
3 and tries to look calm and at ease, but
4 that permanently worried expression on his square open face gives him rather away.
5 The crowds of Americans — clean and well-dressed — stride past him purposefully, unheeding, confident.

6 Henderson walks on. He is nearly forty years old — his birthday is coming up — and just under six feet tall.
7 His frame is sturdy and his face is pleasantly attractive. To his surprise, people are inclined to like him on first acquaintance.
8 He is polite, quite smartly dressed, apart from that slight frown on his forehead:
9 he seems as composed and as unconcerned as, well, as you or me.
10 Henderson has a complaint, a grumble of a deep and insidious nature.
11 He doesn't like himself any more. He's not happy with the personality he's been provided with, thank you very much.
12 Something about him isn't up to scratch, won't do. He'll keep the flesh, but he'd like to do a deal on the spirit, if you've no objections.

13 To the practised observer, everything about him proclaims his Englishness. His haircut — already noted —
14 his pale-lashed eyes, the bloom on his unshaven cheekbones, his blue suit with its double vents in the jacket,
15 the dull worn signet ring on his finger of his left hand, his ankle-length socks (only butlers and chauffeurs wear black),
16 and his shiny, well-creased, toe-capped Oxford shoes.

17 This knowledge — that he is so distinguishable — would distress him, in fact, his grand and only wish
18 is to fit in; to merge and blend with the earnest, enviable people all around him on their way to work.

19 Just another Manhattanite, he tells himself, as he transfers his sabres to his left shoulder, just like everybody else here.

20 He frowns again slightly and shortens his step. He loves America, but will America love him back? Up ahead, the lunatic awaits.

## Translation

1 Observamos a Henderson Dores que sube por Park Avenue en Nueva York — está retrasado, piensa; y
2 lleva sus sables en una bolsa delgada colgada sobre su hombro derecho,
3 y trata de parecer calmado y tranquilo, pero
4 esa expresión constantemente preocupada en su rostro cuadrado y abierto le delata un poco.
5 Las multitudes de americanos — limpios y bien vestidos — pasan junto a él a grandes pasos, con determinación, sin prestarle atención, confiados.

6 Henderson continua andando. Tiene casi cuarenta años — su aniversario está al caer — y mide un poco menos de seis pies de altura.
7 Su complexión es sólida y su cara generosa y agradablemente atractiva. Siempre le sorprende que las gentes se inclinen a simpatizar con él desde el primer encuentro.
8 Es cortés, va bastante bien vestido, y, aparte de ese ligero fruncimiento del ceño,
9 parece tan sereno y tan despreocupado como... como lo podemos estar usted y yo. Pero a Henderson le aqueja un descontento, un rencor, un ruñido de naturaleza profunda e insidiosa.
10 Ha dejado de estimarse; no está contento con la personalidad que se le ha adjudicado, ¡mil gracias!
11 Existe algo dentro de él que no está a la altura, que no le satisface. Se quedará con la envoltura material, ahora bien le gustaría llegar a un pacto respecto al espíritu, si no hay inconveniente.

12 Para un observador experimentado, en efecto, todo en él proclama su condición de inglés. Su corte de pelo — ya descrito —
13 sus ojos bordeados de pestañas claras, el vello sobre sus mejillas sin afeitar, su traje azul con dos aberturas en el chaleco,
14 la sortija de oro mate y usada en el tercer dedo de la mano izquierda, sus calcetines a media pierna de color azul marino (sólo los mayordomos y los choferes llevan negros),
15 y sus zapatos «Oxford» brillantes y arrugados, con puntera reforzada.

16 Este conocimiento — que es tan fácil de reconocer — le perturbaría, debido a que en efecto, su único anhelo
17 es el de encajar, fundirse y mezclarse con la identidad de estas gentes honestas y envidiables que van a trabajar.

18 Justamente ser otro habitante cualquiera de Manhattan, se dice a sí mismo, cambiándose sus sables al hombro izquierdo, como todo el mundo aquí.

19 De nuevo frunce el ceño ligeramente y acorta el paso. Adora a América, pero ¿acaso América le corresponderá? Más allá, el lunático le acecha.

## Lesson notes
- **Park Avenue**: Major north-south avenue in Manhattan, New York City. Note: *New York City* (the city), *New York State* (the state), *NYC* (abbreviation).
- **Washington DC vs. Washington State**: DC (District of Columbia) is the federal district and US capital. Washington State is in the northwest. Capital cities of US states are often not the largest cities (Albany for NY, not NYC; Sacramento for California, not Los Angeles).
- **To stride**: to walk with long, decisive steps. Can be figurative: *Medicine has made great strides* = medicine has advanced significantly.
- **Purpose**: always implies a goal or reason. *Purposefully* = with determination. *On purpose* = intentionally (Aten: *not on purpose* = not intentionally).
- **To frown**: to furrow one's brow in displeasure. *To frown on* = to disapprove of (*Smoking in public is frowned upon*).
- **To bear/hold a grudge against someone**: to harbor resentment. *He bears a grudge against me* = me guarda rencor.
- **Up to scratch**: up to standard, satisfactory. Originally from boxing (ready to fight).
- **To do a deal**: to make an agreement or arrangement.
- **Frame**: can mean the structure of something or a person's build (*a sturdy frame*).
- **Acquaintance**: a person known slightly, not a close friend. *More of an acquaintance than a friend* = más un conocido que un amigo.
- **Different from/to/than**: *from* is most widely accepted; *to* is British; *than* is American. Prefer *from* for safety.

## Vocabulary highlights
- **Sabre**: A type of sword (American English: *saber*)
- **Stride**: A long, confident step; to walk with long steps
- **Frown**: A facial expression of displeasure; to make such an expression
- **Grudge**: A persistent feeling of ill will or resentment
- **Up to scratch**: Meeting required standards
- **Do a deal**: Make an agreement or arrangement
- **Frame**: Body build; structure or skeleton
- **Acquaintance**: A person one knows slightly
- **Square and open**: Describing facial features (broad and honest appearance)
- **At ease**: Comfortable and relaxed
- **Unheeding**: Not paying attention
- **Purposefully**: With determination
- **Pale-lashed**: Having light-colored eyelashes
- **Unshaven**: Not recently shaved
- **Vent**: An opening or slit (in clothing)
- **Signet ring**: A ring with a seal or emblem
- **Ankle-length**: Reaching to the ankles
- **Oxford shoes**: A style of formal lace-up shoes
- **Toe-capped**: Having a reinforced toe section

## Patterns
- Present continuous for narrative effect: *is walking*, *is thinking*
- Appearance vs. reality: *tries to look calm... but... gives him away*
- Conditional: *if you've no objections*
- Contrast between inner feeling and outer appearance
