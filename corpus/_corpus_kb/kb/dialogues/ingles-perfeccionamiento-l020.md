## Original

Twentieth lesson

No news is good news (Part two)

...and that is the end of the national and international news. Now for the sports news, and first, football. Manchester United are preparing for tomorrow night’s cup-tie against Chelsea.

Manager Gordon Bates: — The team are training hard and I think we’ll have a tough match but a fair one.
— What about the question of hooliganism?
— The Government has been very firm about this recently.
— First, I must point out that the team isn’t to blame — and nor are most of the fans. It’s the odd one or two.

— Yes, the public is aware of your recent efforts and the progress that has been made.
— We have our work cut out, but I have some advice for the public: Watch the game and not the fans. — Thank you Mr. Bates.

And now for a quieter game but no less exciting: billiards. Tonight’s international final sees German Walter Glaser against Ireland’s Paddy Mulroney, and we’re going over live to the Dublin Sports Centre for the last minutes.

— And here’s Mulroney lining up his final shot. Oh, he can’t see! The German’s blowing cigar-smoke at him and his hair is in his eyes. And he’s missed. Glaser has won.

To interest an Englishman in war, tell him it is sport; to interest a Frenchman in sport, tell him it is war.

## Translation

Vigésima lección

Las malas noticias son las primeras (Segunda parte)

...Y terminamos así las noticias nacionales e internacionales. Ahora la actualidad deportiva, y en primer lugar el fútbol. El Manchester United se prepara para el partido de copa, mañana por la noche, contra el Chelsea.

El entrenador Gordon Bates: — El equipo entrena duramente y creo que será un partido difícil, pero justo.
— ¿Y qué hay del asunto de los «hooligans» (violencia)? — El Gobierno ha estado muy firme sobre este punto recientemente.
— Primero debo señalar que no se debe culpar al equipo — ni tampoco a la mayoría de los hinchas. Son solo unos pocos.

— Sí, el público es consciente de sus recientes esfuerzos y del progreso que se ha realizado.
— Tenemos trabajo para rato, pero tengo un consejo para el público: observe el partido y no a los hinchas. — Gracias, señor Bates.

Y ahora un juego más tranquilo pero no menos emocionante: el billar. La final internacional de esta noche enfrenta al alemán Walter Glaser con el irlandés Paddy Mulroney, y nos trasladamos en directo al Dublin Sports Centre para los últimos minutos.

— Y aquí está Mulroney afinando su último golpe. ¡Ah! ¡No puede ver! El alemán le echa el humo del puro y sus cabellos le tapan los ojos. Ha fallado... ¡Glaser ha ganado!

Para interesar a un inglés en la guerra, dígale que es deporte; para interesar a un francés en el deporte, dígale que es la guerra.

## Lesson notes

- **And now for...**: Formula used in broadcasting to introduce a new segment. *And now for the weather*.
- **Possessive with time**: *Tomorrow night’s cup-tie*, *yesterday’s match*. Note spelling: *tomorrow’s* (one week’s holiday, four weeks’ holiday).
- **Cup-tie**: Football match in a knockout cup competition. Origin possibly from French *tirer* (to draw lots).
- **Hard**: Adjective and adverb. *Hard job*, *he works hard*. *Hardly* = scarcely (*We hardly see him anymore*).
- **Hooligan**: Violent football fan. Word originates from an Irish family surname (19th century London).
- **Odd one or two**: Few, insignificant number. *The odd cigarette won’t harm you*. *Odds and ends* = miscellaneous items.
- **Progress/advice/work**: Always singular. *He’s made enormous progress*, *I have some advice for you*, *our work is cut out* = we have much work to do.
- **Be to blame**: *The team isn’t to blame* = not responsible.
- **Have work cut out**: Have a difficult task. *She has her work cut out for her*.
- **Go over live**: Broadcast live. *We’re going over live to the stadium*. Pronunciation: /laɪv/.
- **To see (journalistic)**: Present events as active subjects. *Tonight sees Glaser vs. Mulroney*.
- **Hair**: Always singular for a person’s head hair. *His hair is brown*. Plural *hairs* = individual strands.
- **Proverb**: English/French cultural stereotype about sports vs. war.

## Exercises

1. He looks so old that I hardly recognized him.
2. The concert was shown live from Red Square in Moscow.
3. The Government isn’t to blame, and nor are the police.

5. She has her work cut out if she wants to finish by the end of the week.
6. She said she was taking a few odds and ends, and she turned up with four suitcases!

**Fill in the missing words**

2. Tengo un consejo para usted, joven. Sus cabellos son demasiado largos. → I have ______ ______ for you, young man. Your ______ ______ too long.
3. Y ahora, el punto siguiente del orden del día. → ______ ______ ______ the ______ ______.
4. Debo señalar que no sabía nada acerca del asunto. → I ______ ______ ______ that I knew nothing about the affair.
5. El segundo volumen no es menos interesante. → The second volume is ______ ______ ______.

## Vocabulary highlights

- **cup-tie**: knockout match in a tournament
- **hooliganism**: violent behavior by sports fans
- **hooligan**: violent fan
- **Government**: collective noun (can take singular/plural verbs)
- **progress**: advancement (uncountable)
- **advice**: recommendations (uncountable)
- **live**: broadcast in real time
- **hair**: head hair (singular)
- **shot**: attempt to score/goal
- **cigar-smoke**: smoke from a cigar
- **sports centre**: venue for sporting events

## Agent cues

- **Collective nouns**: Note *team are training* (plural focus on individuals) vs. *the team is strong* (singular focus on unit).
- **Broadcast language**: Practice *go over live*, *tonight sees...*.
- **Cultural context**: Understand *hooliganism* in British football history.
- **Humor**: Analyze the irony in *watch the game, not the fans*.
