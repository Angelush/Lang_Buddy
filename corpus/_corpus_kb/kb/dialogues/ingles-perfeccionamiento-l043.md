## Original

9 Ungood will do just as well — because it's an exact opposite, other is not.
10 Or again, if you want a stronger word for good, what use is there in having a whole string of vague useless words like «excellent» and splendid and all the rest?
11 Plusgood covers the meaning; or doubleplusgood if you want something stronger still.
12 In the end, the whole notion of goodness and badness will be covered by only six words — in reality one word.
13 Don't you see the beauty of that, Winston?
14 You don't grasp the beauty of the destruction of words. Do you know that Newspeak is the only language in the world whose vocabulary gets smaller every year?
15 Finally, we shall make thoughtcrime literally impossible, because there will be no words in which to express it.

10 O entonces, Si usted desea una versión más fuerte de bueno, ¿de qué sirve tener una sarta completa de palabras vagas e inútiles como excelente y espléndido y todas las demás?
11 Plusbueno cubre ese sentido; o dobleplusbueno si usted desea algo todavía más fuerte. (...) Finalmente, toda la idea del bien y del mal
12 será cubierta por seis palabras solamente — en realidad una sola palabra. ¿No ve usted la belleza de eso, Winston?
13 Usted no es consciente de la belleza de la destrucción de palabras. ¿Sabe usted que «Newspeak» es el único lenguaje del mundo cuyo vocabulario se restringe todos los años?
14 Al final, terminaremos haciendo literalmente imposible el crimen del pensamiento, debido a que no existirán palabras para expresarlo.

## Lesson notes
- The verb **get** is highly versatile: *get rid of* (discard), *get* as a general verb. Observe *get* + adjective/noun combinations.
- **Whose**: relative possessive pronoun for people (The lady *whose* bag was stolen). Can extend to inanimate objects considered "animated" (a society, a team).
- **Useless**: pronounced /ˈjuːs.ləs/ (IOUSS-LESS). Observe the logic of Newspeak: reducing vocabulary reduces thought range.
- **Subsidiary**: secondary, supplemental. As a noun: a subsidiary company.
- **To rub out**: literally to erase; in slang, to kill (*Lefty was rubbed out* = Lefty was killed).
- **Fewer** vs **less**: *fewer* for countable nouns (fewer words), *less* for uncountable (less water). This distinction is important.
- **String**: a series or sequence (a string of words).

## Exercises
1. — How can I get rid of this cold? — Take an aspirin and go to bed.
2. What use is money if you don't have time to spend it?
3. I only grasp the basics of physics; it's fascinating.
4. It's the only country whose per capita income has risen over the last year.
5. We sell a wide range of products at highly competitive prices.
6. Rub out the figures in the left-hand column and write down these new ones.

Fill in the missing words:
1. Al Capone's gang .......... vice in Chicago. (controlled)
2. Never... .. .. .. to invite him. (has it occurred to me)
3. There are so many relics of the past to b..... ..... of. (get rid)
4. There are ...... cars and ....... pollution since the new law. (fewer, less)
5. Mozart is a composer ..... music is universally liked. (whose)

## Vocabulary highlights
- **Newspeak**: The fictional language of Orwell's *1984*, designed to limit free thought by reducing vocabulary
- **thoughtcrime**: Criminal or unorthodox thoughts; the act of holding unapproved beliefs
- **Ungood/Plusgood/Doubleplusgood**: Newspeak intensity modifiers (bad/very good/extremely good)
- **Get rid of**: Phrasal verb meaning to discard, eliminate, or remove
- **Vague**: Not clearly or explicitly stated; unclear
- **Useless**: Having no practical use or value
- **Notion**: An idea, concept, or belief about something
- **Cover**: To include, encompass, or deal with
- **Subsidiary**: Secondary, supplemental, or supporting
- **String**: A series or sequence of similar things

## Patterns
- **Possessive relative clause**: *whose* + noun for possession (*the man whose car was stolen*)
- **Rhetorical question**: *What use is there in...?* to question purpose or value
- **Newspeak intensifiers**: *plusgood*, *doubleplusgood* for graded intensity
- **Phrasal verb**: *get rid of* for elimination

## Agent cues
Focus on the logical structure of controlled language. Practice: (1) relative clauses with *whose*, (2) phrasal verb *get rid of*, (3) distinguishing *fewer* vs *less*. Note how Orwell uses language to illustrate thought control — a key theme of dystopian literature. Pay attention to the pronunciation of *useless* (/ˈjuːs.ləs/).
