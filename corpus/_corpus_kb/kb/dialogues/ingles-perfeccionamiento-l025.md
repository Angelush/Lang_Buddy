## Original

Twenty-fifth lesson

Let’s carry on

Let’s carry on for one more lesson with these extremely useful phrasal verbs.

— Do you have anything on this evening? — Afraid so. I’m seeing my lawyer.
— No, I won’t lend them any more money. I’ve helped them out enough already.
— We were held up for an hour at the airport with those damned security checks.

Keep on revising: it’s the only way to learn.

Public demand has fallen off and the factory has had to lay off three hundred workers.
Sunday is the only day of the week I can lie in, so don’t call me before eleven o’clock.

— If you want to go to the movies, I’ll look after the kids for you.
— Damn! I’ve forgotten his number. — Well, look it up in the phone book.

The bomb blew up in a crowded shopping centre, killing three people and wounding twenty others.
The fight was called off because one of the boxers was suffering from a bad cold!

I came across this photograph of my parents’ Silver Wedding when I was going through my drawers.
— I’m afraid I don’t have any butter. You’ll have to make do with margarine.
— I just dropped in to see how you were. Nowadays, I rarely leave the office before seven thirty.
— When did you give up smoking? — Three days, two hours, four minutes and twenty two seconds ago!
— I’m sorry, I didn’t understand the last part of your explanation. Could you go over it again, please?

There was a loud bang and all the lights went out. I thought a fuse had blown, but the house had been struck by lightning!
— Hang on a minute! You’re going too fast.

## Translation

Vigésima quinta lección

Continuemos

Continuemos durante una lección más con estos *phrasal verbs* extremadamente útiles.

— ¿Tiene usted algo previsto para esta noche? — Desgraciadamente sí. Veo a mi abogado.
— No, no les prestaré más dinero. Ya les he ayudado bastante.
— Nos retuvieron durante una hora en el aeropuerto debido a esos malditos controles de seguridad.

Siga repasando: es la única manera de aprender.

La demanda pública ha bajado y la fábrica ha tenido que despedir a trescientos obreros.
El domingo es el único día de la semana en que puedo quedarme en la cama, así que no me llame antes de las once.

— Si quieres ir al cine, cuidaré de los niños por ti.
— ¡Caramba! He olvidado su número. — Bueno, búscalo en la guía telefónica.

La bomba estalló en un centro comercial atestado, matando a tres personas e hiriendo a otras veinte.
El combate fue cancelado porque uno de los boxeadores tenía un fuerte resfriado.

Encontré esta foto de las bodas de plata de mis padres cuando estaba revisando mis cajones.
— Lo siento, no tengo mantequilla. Tendrá que arreglárselas con margarina.
— Solo pasé para ver cómo estabas. Últimamente, rara vez salgo de la oficina antes de las siete y media.
— ¿Cuándo dejó de fumar? — ¡Hace tres días, dos horas, cuatro minutos y veintidós segundos!
— Lo siento, no entendí la última parte de su explicación. ¿Podría repetirla, por favor?

Hubo un ruido fuerte y todas las luces se apagaron. Creí que se había fundido un fusible, ¡pero el rayo había caído sobre la casa!
— ¡Espere un momento! Va usted muy rápido.

## Lesson notes

- **Have anything on?**: Have plans. *What’s on?* = what events are happening (theater listings etc.).
- **Seeing my lawyer**: *See* can take continuous form when meaning *meeting someone*. Normally, verbs like *see* don’t use continuous (*I see the problem*), but for meetings, it’s acceptable (*I’m seeing my lawyer at 3*).
- **Help out**: Give temporary assistance. *They helped me out with a loan* = gave me a loan in a time of need.
- **Damned**: Originally a strong religious exclamation (*damned soul*). Now milder but still avoid with sensitive people.
- **Look up**: Find information in a reference book/directory. *Phone book* = telephone directory. *Directory Enquiries* (GB) / *Directory Assistance* (US) = phone information service.
- **Lay off**: Dismiss workers temporarily/permanently. *The factory laid off 300 workers*.
- **Lie in**: Stay in bed late. *I like to lie in on Sundays*.
- **Look after**: Take care of. *Look after the kids* = babysit.
- **Drop in**: Visit informally. Slightly more familiar than *call in* but often interchangeable.
- **Give up**: Stop a habit. *Give up smoking* = quit smoking.
- **Go over**: Review, repeat. *Go over it again* = explain again.
- **Fuse/blown**: *A fuse* = safety device in electrical circuits. *Blow* = explode (past *blew*, *blown*). *The house was struck by lightning* = hit by lightning.
- **Hang on**: Wait a moment. *Hang on a minute!* = wait!

## Exercises

**Sentence practice**
1. — What do we have on this morning, Bill? — A technical meeting at ten.
2. Four armed men held up a security guard and robbed him of £4000.
3. They had to call off the rehearsal because the director was ill.
4. Can you make do without me for ten minutes? I have to go to the bank.
5. Hang on a minute! Not too fast!

**Fill in the missing words**
1. La empresa Barnham ha despedido a trescientos obreros. → Barnham’s ______ ______ three hundred workers.
2. Continúe tomando los medicamentos; pronto se mejorará. → ______ ______ taking the medicine; you’ll soon get better.
3. Mi abogado se ocupa de mis intereses financieros. → My lawyer ______ ______ my financial interests.
4. Si no conoce la palabra, búsquela en el diccionario. → If you don’t know the word ______ ______ ______ in the dictionary.
5. Consideremos de nuevo su explicación. → ______ ______ ______ explanation again.

**Answers**: 1. has laid off, 2. Carry on, 3. looks after, 4. look it up, 5. Let’s go over

## Agent cues

- **Phrasal verb mastery**: Practice all verbs in context. Note which are separable (*look it up*) vs. inseparable (*give up smoking*).
- **Pronoun placement**: For separable phrasal verbs, pronouns go between verb and particle (*look it up*).
- **Daily routines**: Use *lie in*, *look after*, *drop in* in personal narratives.
- **Problem-solving**: *Make do*, *help out*, *carry on* express resilience.
