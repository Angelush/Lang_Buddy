## Original

Sixteenth lesson

Little words mean so much

This lesson will require a little patience — and a lot of indulgence, since the use of prepositions is often arbitrary and the only way to ensure being correct is often to accept without asking why! Look at the examples below and look for the difference in meaning between the two forms in each sentence.

— You can ask me all the questions you want but don’t ask me for money.
— He said that he had been working late at the office but I didn’t believe him. I don’t believe in fairies!
— I think of her all the time. — What do you think about the plan to build a by-pass around the village?
— Excuse me sir, we’re closing. Would you mind paying for your drinks? — I’ll get this, Mike. You paid the bill in the café.

The security guards searched the suitcases thoroughly — they even used sniffer dogs. Apparently, they were searching for drugs.
— ...and we remind members of the audience not to shout the answer. — Don’t shout at your children, try and reason with them.
The manager has been sick with the ’flu for a week. — I’m sick of wasting time in traffic jams. I’ll take the Tube.
He’s a terrible hunter: he shoots at anything that moves. Last week, he shot one of the beaters in the foot.

When he was young, he used to dream of becoming president. — Last night I dreamed of the house we used to spend our holidays in.

Ah! Did you notice that we ended that sentence with a preposition? Some writers say that this is ‘incorrect’ but it is also the most common form of expression — and far less formal than the alternative.
Look: Here’s the article about which I was telling you, or: Here’s the article which I was telling you about. Or even more simply: Here’s the article I was telling you about. Don’t worry! Nothing else disappears.

In our next lesson, we will see some more examples of this simple and idiomatic way of constructing sentences.
A zealous speech-writer once modified one of Winston Churchill’s speeches to ‘tidy up’ any prepositions at the ends of sentences. Churchill was a great wielder of the English language and this overly formal style annoyed him. Changing all his sentences back to their original form, prepositions and all, he growled:
— This is the kind of English up with which I will not put!

## Translation

Decimo sexta lección

Las pequeñas palabras son muy importantes

Esta lección requerirá un poco de paciencia — y mucha flexibilidad, ya que la utilización de las preposiciones es algunas veces arbitraria y la mejor manera de garantizar que se está en lo correcto es, a menudo, aceptar sin preguntar.
— Usted puede hacerme todas las preguntas que quiera pero no me pida dinero.
— Él dijo que trabajaba hasta tarde en la oficina pero yo no le creí. ¡No creo en cuentos de hadas!
— Pienso en ella todo el tiempo. — ¿Qué piensa sobre el proyecto para construir una circunvalación alrededor de la ciudad?
— Perdóneme, señor, vamos a cerrar... ¿Le importaría pagar sus consumiciones? — Soy yo el que invita, Mike. Tú pagaste la cuenta en el café.

Los guardias de seguridad examinaron las maletas a fondo — incluso emplearon los perros rastreadores. Aparentemente, ellos buscaban drogas.
— ...y le recordamos al público no gritar la respuesta. — No riña a sus niños, intente razonar con ellos.
El director estuvo una semana con gripe. — Estoy harto de perder el tiempo en atascos de tráfico. Tomaré el metro.
Es un mal cazador: dispara a cualquier cosa que se mueve. La semana pasada le disparó en un pie a un batidor.

Cuando él era joven, soñaba con convertirse en presidente. — Anoche soñé con la casa donde pasábamos nuestras vacaciones.

¡Ah! ¿Se dio cuenta de que terminamos esa frase con una preposición? Ciertos escritores dicen que esto es ‘incorrecto’, pero es también la forma de expresión más común — y mucho menos formal que la alternativa.
Mire: Aquí está el artículo del que le he hablado. O, aún más simplemente: Aquí está el artículo del cual le hablé. ¡No se preocupe! Nada más desaparece.

En nuestra próxima lección, veremos algunos otros ejemplos de esta manera simple e idiomática de construir las frases.
Un afamado redactor, en una ocasión, modificó un discurso de Winston Churchill para ‘poner en orden’ las preposiciones al final de las frases. Churchill manejaba formidablemente la lengua inglesa y aquel estilo exageradamente formal le molestó. Cambiando todas las frases a su forma original, incluyendo las preposiciones, gruñó:
— ¡Esta es la clase de inglés que no toleraré!

## Lesson notes

- **Believe vs. believe in**: You *believe* someone (accept their words as true) but you *believe in* God, fairies, or abstract concepts. *I don’t believe in fairies!* is ironic, implying disbelief in the speaker’s intelligence.
- **Think of vs. think about**: Often interchangeable, but *think about* implies reflection over time (*Think about it for a bit*).
- **Pay vs. pay for**: You *pay* a person or a bill directly (*I’ll pay the bill*), but *pay for* a service or object (*I’ll pay for the drinks*).
- **Search vs. search for**: You *search* a place or person (*The guards searched the suitcases*), but *search for* a specific item (*They were searching for drugs*).
- **Be sick with vs. be sick of**: *Sick with* an illness (*sick with the flu*), *sick of* being tired of something (*sick of traffic jams*).
- **Shoot at**: Direction is implied; *shoot at* a target, *shoot* the target if hit. *Shot in the foot* = injured by gunfire.
- **Used to**: Indicates past habit (*He used to dream of becoming president*).
- **Dream of**: British English allows both *dreamed* and *dreamt*; American English uses *dreamed*.
- **Tube**: Familiar term for the London Underground; *subway* in New York.
- **Put up with**: Informal for *tolerate*. Churchill’s sarcastic *up with which I will not put* highlights the absurdity of strict preposition rules.

## Exercises

**Sentence practice**
1. The laboratory has asked for more samples. I wonder why.
2. I’ve hunted everywhere for those notes, but I can’t find them.
3. He tripped over a fallen branch and shot himself in the leg.
4. If we follow the A26, we can by-pass the town centre.
5. Would you mind finishing your drink? I’m closing.
6. The dog whimpered in pain.

**Fill in the missing words**
1. — Do you believe in God? No, I’m an atheist. — Do you ______ ______ God? — No, I’m an atheist.
2. The hunter ______ ______ the boar.
3. Leave it. I’m the one paying. Here, I’ll ______ ______ this.
4. What do you ______ ______ the new secretary?
5. This is the kind of English which I will not ______ ______ ______.

**Answers**: 1. believe in, 2. shot at, 3. pay for, 4. think of, 5. put up with

## Vocabulary highlights

- **fairy**: mythical creature; *believe in fairies* = accept magical thinking
- **sniffer dog**: dog trained to detect drugs/explosives
- **Tube**: London Underground system
- **’flu**: colloquial for *influenza*
- **beater**: person who flushes out game for hunters
- **bypass**: road built around a town to avoid congestion
- **put up with**: tolerate (informal)

## Patterns

| Form | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| believe in | accept existence/validity of abstract concept | I don’t believe in fairies |
| think of | consider briefly | I think of her all the time |
| think about | reflect on | Think about the plan |
| pay for | purchase/remunerate for service | Pay for your drinks |
| search for | look for specific item | Searching for drugs |
| shoot at | direct fire toward | He shot at the boar |
| put up with | tolerate | I can’t put up with his rudeness |

## Agent cues

- **Preposition focus**: Note how prepositions change verb meaning (e.g., *look* vs. *look for*).
- **Phrasal verb separation**: Object pronouns go between verb and preposition (*put it off*).
- **Idiomatic expressions**: Practice *be sick of*, *put up with*, *shoot at*.
- **Contrastive analysis**: Compare English prepositional logic with Spanish equivalents.
